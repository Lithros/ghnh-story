Jean-Luc Aveline vs. Godefroy de Beaufosse

March 1624 · the doorstep of Alida Demachy, Paris · Cause: rival suitors publicly declared for the same lady · sabre against cutlass

The first duel of the game, fought in the first week of the first month, before either man had so much as joined a regiment. Two newcomers to Paris arrived at the same door with the same bouquet-borne intentions — and the campaign’s five-year tradition of monthly bloodshed began.

Editorial note: quoted text is verbatim from the game record; Discord @-mentions inside quotations are rendered as the character’s name.

The quarrel

Narrator — “Godefroy de Beaufosse had his sights set on the more respectable of the Demachy twins, Alida. He had everything in order – boots shined, shirt freshly laundered, and a gorgeous bouquet tucked under his arm – as he made his way up the avenue towards her apartments. Much to his shock, he found none other before her door than Jean-Luc Aveline, similarly appointed for courting. The two men stared at one another with suspicion. Their intentions were as obvious as they were mutually exclusive. Alida, standing in the doorway, clutched her hands to her breast, awaiting to learn which man’s love was strongest. Her sister interrupted preparations for her own courting to stare down from her own window, and a crowd begin to gather around the men.”

Narrator — “Does anyone dispute that this is cause for a duel?”

The crowd’s answer came back with four crossed-swords reactions and not one dissenting voice.

Phillipe du Kaneda — “This is not some Court of Solomon. The lady’s heart cannot be divided. It can only be won through a test of courage.”

Curtis Sinnoch — “As newcomers and friends they arrived, but have thusly discovered they yern for the same fair maiden! Let them settle this with a duel, and let us all see who is the quality of man who will stand and deliver! Let them duel!”

Guillaume d’Avergny — “Hear, hear! Be valiant, disappoint not the lady!”

Du Kaneda backed his enthusiasm with coin:

Phillipe du Kaneda — “/subtly taps a purse of 25 Crowns and points to Jean-Luc Aveline as his man for the first non-combatant interested in a spectator’s wager/”

No taker stepped forward before the blades came out, and the purse went back into his coat. Meanwhile Aveline eyed his rival’s bouquet:

Jean-Luc Aveline — “I do hope that is a bouquet of yellow roses or spiderwort! Judging your intent, they will soon be speckled crimson!”

Pierre de Tête — “say the word, draw your sword, screw your courage to the sticking place.”

De Beaufosse, a gentleman lately come from rougher country, answered the challenge late that night — and named a most rural weapon:

Godefroy de Beaufosse — “A duel it must be! This is, of course, the only way to satisfactorily resolve any dispute of importance. I will have you know, Jean-Luc Aveline, that I have spent some time in more rural environs where tempers run high, and I am fully prepared to provide a commensurate response! Here, I draw my cutlass!”

Jean-Luc Aveline — “We can commence!”

In the reckoning of expertise, the Narrator ruled that Godefroy held the advantage. It would not help him.

Leon Mandelbrot vs. Etienne de Mercoeur

March 1624 · the doorstep of Adélaïde Blaise, Paris · Cause: rival suitors for the same lady · rapier against rapier

The second duel of the game, fought the day after Aveline and Beaufosse crossed blades over the Demachy twins — and the first true test of the dueling rules. Where the first affair ended in six turns on a shattered cutlass, this one ran nineteen turns of rapier-play, five wounds against three, and left both suitors standing in their own blood before the lady’s window. It set the standard every later duel would be measured against, and it delivered the campaign’s first great lesson in the cruelty of seventeenth-century romance: the lady wanted a corpse.

The quarrel

Etienne de Mercoeur stood behind, well-bred eyes narrowed at Leon’s impertinence. Young Adélaïde peered out from the window, and the men knew they were at an impasse. A crowd began to form.”

Both men — a friendship sealed in blood and an engagement at the Hunter’s.

Pierre de Tête vs. Jean-Luc Aveline

April 1624 · the Frog & Peach, Paris · Cause: enemy regiments — a Dragoon major and a Cuirassier private under one roof · sabre against sabre

The third duel of the game, and the first fought not over a lady but over regimental colors. In the first week of April 1624 both men put on a uniform: Pierre de Tête bought his majority in Grand Duke Max’s Dragoons, and Jean-Luc Aveline — victor of the game’s very first duel — enlisted as a humble private in the Archduke Leopold Cuirassiers. The two regiments despise one another, and by the code of the game, when members of unfriendly regiments meet, honor demands blood. Two weeks later, they met.

The quarrel

Jean-Luc Aveline — “Jean-Luc Aveline, reporting for duty!”

Narrator — “The subaltern on duty hands you a small pamphlet detailing the history and standards of the regiment. You have been assigned to the 3rd Squadron, F Troop, under the command of Roland Hodin, a dashing and brilliant young man.”

Jean Shawinigan vs. Louis Laveuse

January 1628 · Red Phillips gambling club, Paris · Cause: Mlle. Adélaïde Blaise — won and lost in a day · sabre against rapier

The thirteenth duel of the game, the third fought on the long-suffering floorboards of Red Phillips, and the first ever fought by the player line that had produced Phillipe du Kaneda, Hercule Bonaparte, and Sébastien Lefebvre — four characters and nearly four real years without once crossing steel. The man who finally broke the streak was Jean Shawinigan: outwardly a disowned merchant’s son of impeccable calm, actually a time traveler stranded in the 1600s with a neural link, implanted sobriety meds, and an anti-aging regimen. Across from him stood Louis Laveuse, “the bastard spawn of the Earl of Ambroise and a washerwoman” — the newest character of the player whose Robert d’Engaux had won the previous duel at this very club, then died at La Rochelle holding the affections of Adélaïde Blaise. Louis re-courted his predecessor’s lady and won her; the next day Jean bought a private festival and took her away. The gossips of Paris said she chose Louis “for about an hour.” The duel that settled it lasted eleven rounds, ended in the worst single stroke the club had ever hosted, and produced the most seventeenth-century last words in the whole chronicle.
