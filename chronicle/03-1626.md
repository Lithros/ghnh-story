# Chapter III — 1626: The Year of the Thrown Cutlass

> *"Death is fairly conclusive."*
> — Colonel Marius Thibodeaux, Royal Marines

## I. Snake eyes (January)

The Rochant–Baschet affair came to its point in the first month of the year, and its point was one inch of flung steel.

Jean Rochant had hunted the painter methodically. Amand Baschet's devotion to sabre practice at the Grand Duke Max's Dragoons headquarters had become so well known it made him findable, and one January morning Rochant stood at the stable gates, hand on weapon, fury in his eyes, before the whole regiment's grooms and privates. Baschet — who wished aloud that he had gone with Adélaïde to visit her great-aunt instead — tried everything the age of reason offered: he mock-recruited Rochant into the Dragoons; he flattered his overlooked talents; he offered him, openly, a captaincy with the fee and the horse paid — *"There are no loans between friends!"*

*"I am perfectly proud of my position in the Royal Marines,"* said Rochant. *"A position I earned through battling for France, while you were gallivanting around Paris stealing another man's mistress."* And he drew.

What followed was the most infuriating duel in the annals of Paris. Baschet — a self-declared Renaissance Man dragged into "distasteful neanderthal nonsense" — fought twenty-three rounds without shedding or losing a drop of blood: jump-backs, blocks, rests, and an unbroken stream of hospitality. Would Jean care for lunch? A salad? Perhaps a painting session afterward? *"Dodging a duel before first blood has been drawn,"* howled Rochant, chasing him around the yard, *"I thought the Dragoons employed brave, honorable men!"*

On the twenty-fourth round, Rochant threw his cutlass.

The reader will recall the law of thrown weapons in this world: a hurled cutlass kills instantly on double ones — and on anything else accomplishes nothing but disarming its owner. It was a one-in-thirty-six gesture of pure exasperation. The dice came up **1 and 1**.

> *"The weapon carves a perfect arc in the air before embedding itself directly in Amand's throat. Amand falls to the ground, instantly dead."*

**Amand Baschet, 1609–1626** — *never threw a fight.* The killer stood over the body genuinely appalled at himself — *"Alas! I did not want this, but his hubris has pushed me past my limits!"* — collected six status points and a lesson in expertise, and walked home to Laurence Caillat, the lady he had won weeks before on the strength of the hatred with which he spoke the name *Baschet*. (Mlle. Blaise's father, it was said, had always wanted a military man in the family; he now had a vacancy.) The deceased took it better than anyone: his shade was observed to make ghost sounds — *"OOOOHHHHHH, at last now I can do something fun with my day"* — and float off toward the art gallery.

> 🎲 **At the table.** This was, by unanimous acclamation, the dice moment of the campaign: a duel deliberately stalled into farce (Baschet's player knew her pacifist painter could not win a fair exchange and plotted twelve-move programs made entirely of evasion), broken by the lowest-probability roll in the dueling rules. The month's resolution had been drawn out across multiple delayed sessions — *"Weeks One through Three pass without incident. THERE IS BLOODSHED ON WEEK 4!"* — until the spectators were making Dragon Ball Z pacing jokes ("NEXT TIME, ON DRAGON BALL Z"; "That was like 5 damn episodes"). Then snake eyes, and the screaming. The tombstone went up with a laughing-face reaction on it. Its owner rolled her *third* character — the death-count race with the table's reigning champion ("Catching up to me in # of chars" — "Much sooner than anticipated!") was now properly on.

## II. The winter accounts (January–February)

The winter campaign of 1625–26 was, by the standards of this war, housekeeping: with Soubise in chains and the Protestant strongholds ordered demilitarized, the army swept the west for traitor militias. On the banks of the Sèvre Niortaise the Royal Foot Guards caught a Protestant contingent waiting for the ferry, and Colonel Bonaparte ordered a no-quarter flanking assault, reasoning aloud that *"the memory of what happens to traitors will be forever etched into their minds."* Near-total annihilation. The rewards cascaded down the victorious chain: another Mention for Hercule and four hundred crowns of Protestant payroll; a fifth Mention, a lieutenant-colonelcy, and seven hundred crowns for Curtis Sinnoch; a brevet majority and five hundred crowns for Jules Lavelle.

The campaign claimed one gentleman of note, and on his own terms. Alphonse Devereux — elephant-rider, colonel of the Royal Marines, one of the last four founders, a man rich enough to keep three named horses and smug enough to name them Roach, Cheval, and Traduc — had gone to the winter war without his Marines, his regiment declining to accompany him. ("Alphonse has gone to war by himself," the record notes; "C'est la vie," answered Paris.) He threw himself at the enemy with a recklessness no arithmetic could excuse, and the record gives him the whole of his obituary in one parenthesis:

> *"(Alphonse elected to go out in a blaze of glory.)"*

A musketball between the eyes; found later, face-down in the mud. His death left the Royal Marines' 1st Battalion without a commander — a vacancy into which, within weeks, climbed a new man on borrowed money: **Marius Thibodeaux**, fourth of his player's line, who had joined the Marines under Devereux mere weeks before ("That's a sweet MA! Join us, and fight for France!"), and who now announced with disarming honesty, *"I know it looks bad, but I would like to purchase a Majority!"* His business plan, stated for the record: *"I figure I will either win glory (and gold) in battle, or die trying anyway."*

Of the ten founders of March 1624, one now remained: Curtis Sinnoch.

## III. Something in the air (March–May)

Spring 1626 opened with a day the courting registers still speak of. Two men needed a natural six between one sunrise and the next. Marius Thibodeaux, rebuffed the month before by the exalted Angélique Beauchamp ("her heart belongs to another"), returned without so much as an extravagant gift — and rolled his six. That night Clemens Charron, whose muscle-flexing courtship technique had failed with *every* lady in Paris across two years — four consecutive months of it at poor Suzanne LeBeau, once "nigh-continuously for the entirety of the morning" until he collapsed in a dust-pile, once via backflip-with-a-twist — flexed *Very Hard* at the sixteen-year-old heiress Alida Rodier, wealthy, influential, and impossible. Six. The Narrator surveyed his own dice in wonder: *"There must be something in the air this spring."* The peasant ox had landed the richest débutante in France. *"Man's a war hero now,"* his backers explained. *"Counts for something."* — *"I got fun new scars!"*

The same season, Jules Lavelle quietly abandoned the faithful Eugénie Landry — she learned of it only when he missed their weekly appointment; "Such a shame," said the Narrator, in the tone of a man closing a ledger — to court the much-traded Adélaïde Blaise. *"Poor Blaise,"* observed Curtis from a safe distance, *"really getting tossed around."*

The armies, meanwhile, went to the mountains. France's answer to the Habsburg vise was the **Valtellina expedition**: an allied siege, with Grisons, Venice and Savoy, of the fortified town of Poschiavo, undertaken by a coalition whose members had each assumed the *others* were bringing the siege equipment. The colonel advising the assault — an "arrogant blowhard... in flight from gambling arrears" — proposed going "right up the middle." Jules Lavelle's 2nd Battalion, which had spent the training weeks "climbing bridges around Paris," was repelled from the walls by a hail of thrown rocks. The town fell anyway: Marius Thibodeaux smashed the gate's lock with the hilt of his cutlass, and Curtis Sinnoch's 1st Battalion cut down the Spaniards hauling flaming grease to the parapet with a single snapped volley. Then it rained money: gems pried from the town's coat of arms, the papal treasury, pillage by the hundredweight — eleven hundred crowns to Jules, eighteen hundred to Jean Rochant, something for everyone.

And at the accounting afterward, Curtis Sinnoch's sixth Mention in Despatches tipped the scales that had jammed twice before: **Sir Curtis Sinnoch**, knight of France, at last. The arithmetic of his climb — Social Level 14 by summer, the highest in Paris — gave fresh point to the honest lament he had first uttered as a mere major: *"Damnit, I need some Lt. Colonels and Colonels to go ahead and die already!"* It was, as he had also observed, lonely at the top.

## IV. A friendly duel (June–July)

With the armies away at the summer's sieges, the two knights-errant of the Royal Foot Guards found themselves garrisoning Paris and bored. Curtis proposed the solution of the age: a *friendly* duel — to half-blood only, no thrown weapons, staged on one of Bothwell's manicured lawns. *"Something to keep our wits sharp and our reflexes sharper... We want this to be respectful!"*

Friends at the front demanded letters and odds by carrier pigeon. Jules, the record notes, "tries not to focus on Curtis's bulging biceps." For twelve rounds it was sporting — a thirty-two-point slash answered by needling little cuts and acrobatic retreats — until, on the thirteenth, Curtis closed the distance, took Jules by the collar, and brought his knee up into his descending chin.

> *"The sound of broken ivory can be heard clear across the yard, and shards of shattered teeth spill in a bloody stream from Jules' slack jaw."*

*"Glory... to... the Royal... Foot... uhghhhghghh,"* said Jules, and slept. Curtis — who mid-bout had shrugged off Jules's best cut of the evening with *"Ah! Got me! Well done, Jules! Though I think I've injured myself worse shaving!"* — apologized handsomely for the finish (*"I appear to have over-done myself!"*) and dutifully lost a status point for beating a friend. Jules, in time, acquired a gleaming replacement set: *"I am now the proud bearer of a mouthful of beaver's teeth!"*

> 🎲 **At the table.** The friendly duel resolved, in perfect cosmic alignment, on the GM's real birthday — Valentine's Day 2021 — prompting the birthday greeting *"For his birthday he got his teeth kicked in!"* The official month-announcement folded life into fiction with a single line that connoisseurs of the announcements channel treasure: **"July 1626 passes without incident, other than a massive dental bill for Jules."** (The Gazette, players were informed, would remain on hiatus "until my kid is born" — a pause the game-calendar rendered as roughly eighteen months of in-world silence, and the reason this chronicle leans on regimental gossip for the middle years.)

The summer's true campaign was political. Cardinal Richelieu accused the Comte de Chalais of plotting his assassination; the conspirators sheltered at Rennes under the Duke of Vendôme, the King's own half-brother; and a royal task force went to fetch them out, commanded by General Michael Masse, "known for his reliability, decorum, and above all, luxurious moustaches." The general's pre-assault ride along the lines was entirely inaudible, "but his moustaches are rather inspiring." The Picardy Musketeers stormed the walls; the army's finest foot commander, Brigadier Vasseur, charged through the gates first and alone, and was pulled from his horse and bayoneted; and the new man Guerinet Durand — third of the Soucy line, a wealthy merchant's second son — came through "among the first non-suicidal troops through the gate," collecting a Mention and fourteen hundred crowns. *"This second son has something to rub in his older brother's face!!!!"* Rennes fell; the conspirators went in irons to Nantes; Marius Thibodeaux emerged a full **Colonel of the Royal Marines**, well under a year after joining as a captain with an apology.

One officer conspicuously did not march that summer. Hercule Bonaparte, emerging from two months' seclusion with Alida to dispatch the Royal Foot Guards to the front, was caught at the gates of Paris by a communiqué from the Minister of War: the Guards would stay to defend the capital against "heavy Habsburg maneuvering." Hercule turned his carriage around and spent the month, the record tells us, planning a different campaign entirely:

> *"If the Minister of War refuses his brilliance, then the only solution is to become the Minister of War."*

## V. The cabbage fields of Andorra (October–November)

The year's last campaign began as a diplomatic errand and ended as a legend. Cardinal Richelieu traveled secretly to Andorra to negotiate peace with Spain, escorted by the Frontier Regiment, the Royal Foot Guards, and the Royal Marines. The valley fields were eerily silent as the column passed — until a farmer straightened from his row, threw down his hoe, pulled an arquebus from the cabbages, and screamed:

> **"FOR SOUBISE!"**

An ambush — English-financed, as the interrogations would show, the first open evidence that Albion had moved from lending France ships to buying France's rebels. It failed spectacularly. The Frontier colonel's roared fire-order broke the ambuscade; Hercule's staggered firing lines shredded "the remaining English cowards"; the Cardinal, unscratched, personally handed Bonaparte a four-hundred-crown treasury note. The honors flowed like the wine after: a fifth Mention and a brevet *brigadier-generalcy* for Hercule — technically, he was delighted to learn, a step *down* in social precedence from Colonel of the Royal Foot Guards, though "there are perks to being the King's BFF" — and for Jules Lavelle, who had ridden into the guns with deliberate recklessness, a Mention, the Key to the City of Andorra, and the sixth pip on the die that mattered: **Sir Jules Lavelle**, knight of France, dental prosthetics and all. His verdict on the day has the ring of a man who means it: *"War truly is the most glorious endeavor known to man."* Sir Curtis Sinnoch, needing anything but a fifteen to die, rolled a twelve and recorded for posterity: *"Let me just change into new shorts..."*

But the cabbage fields collected a toll. Jean Rochant's 1st Battalion was caught flat in the opening volley — his colonel and friend Marius "leading by not really leading at all" — and Rochant, an orphan who had refused every loan ever offered him (*"Never! Loans killed my father!"*), who had clawed from private to a paid majority, who had avenged himself on Baschet with the most improbable throw in the history of fencing, chose recklessness one more time. *"Welp... C'est la mort."*

> *"An English bullet lodges in Jean's side. He continues fighting valiantly, earning great renown for himself. He is found after the battle, having bled out, his cutlass buried in one last foe."*

**Jean Rochant, 1605–1626** — *a fish out of water.* His commander's eulogy was the shortest on record: *"Death is fairly conclusive."*

> 🎲 **At the table.** Rochant's death nearly ended more than a character. Three days after the resolution his player posted, plaintively, *"Do I not get a Gravestone?"* — ("Hush, dead man," said the table) — and the GM, promising the stone was coming, answered with unusual candor: *"I'm still processing your death."* The player did not roll a new character for months. It is the only death in the record that made the *Narrator* grieve — and the stone, when it came, bore the fishing pun its three 🎣 reactions demanded. Dice Golem, it should be noted for the defense, was *down* the night of the Andorra resolution; every fatal roll was made on an external site. The bot's alibi is airtight.

The year closed quietly, which in this Paris meant: Hercule handed the Royal Foot Guards' chair to Curtis with the valediction *"Don't redecorate the office just yet, but enjoy the chair — I don't intend to be coming back, if it can be helped"*; the third proclamation of new titles went up in the peerage rolls; and Sir Jules Lavelle sat down at the Hunter's dice table in December and did not lose a single round all night — four wins, three house auto-cuts, two prudent cuts of his own, plus eight status and three hundred crowns — the first installment of a legend the croupiers of Hunter's would come to regard as a natural disaster.

Below the gilding, the war had changed shape. Soubise was a prisoner, but his name was a battle cry in Spanish valleys; his Republic still held the western ports; and English money was now arriving faster than English apologies. The new year would bring the English themselves.
