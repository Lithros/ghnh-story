# Prologue — The City of Great Hearts

> *"Will you stand for money? For love? Or for honor and glory? Whatever your desire, the streets run red with chances to obtain it."*
> — the invitation that began it all, March 1624

Paris, in the spring of 1624, was the finest and most dangerous city in the world, and the young men who poured into it knew both facts and cared only about the first.

They came from everywhere and from every rank of the great chain of being: second sons of wealthy merchants with no inheritance and everything to prove; bastards of earls, paid a stipend to *be somewhere else*; sons of counts with letters of introduction; a peasant's boy with the shoulders of an ox; even a Dutch banker with an unpronounceable name and impeccable manners. What they shared was young blood and the same arithmetic — that in Paris, a man's standing was a thing that could be *built*, month by month, out of exactly four materials: the club he caroused in, the regiment whose colors he wore, the lady whose favor he kept, and the men he was willing to kill politely at dawn.

This chronicle is the history of some five years of that arithmetic — from March 1624 to the frozen campaign of early 1629 — assembled from the archives of the *Gazette de France*, the regimental rolls, the ledgers of five gentlemen's clubs and one discreet establishment called the Maison Le Fourcy, the minutes of a great many duels, and the melancholy registry of a certain cemetery whose headstones grew wittier as they grew more numerous.

It is a history of great hearts and noble hearts, which are not always the same thing.

## The world, briefly

A gentleman's life in this Paris ran on a monthly clock. Each month he had funds to spend or squander, and a **Social Level** — the single number that determined which doors opened to him — to maintain or climb. Status was earned by being *seen*: carousing at a club fine enough to matter, escorting a mistress beautiful or exalted enough to be envied, toadying to greater men, winning at the tables with enough grace, and above all by glory in arms. Status unspent evaporated; rent came due; tailors were not patient. A man who could not keep up appearances fell, and Paris watched him fall.

The clubs formed a ladder of their own. At the bottom stood **Red Phillip's**, a club in barest name only, with all the rustic charm of a dockside tavern, a toothless proprietor, a narcoleptic fiddler, and floorboards rumored to contain more human blood than wood. Above it, the **Frog & Peach**, founded by a retired whaler, all scrimshaw and children underfoot; then the **Blue Gables**, whose original blue gables burned down decades ago; then **Hunter's**, where the talk was of ministers and the Cardinal's stag's head watched the dice table with cold glass eyes; and at the summit **Bothwell's**, of which little can be printed, since discretion was the entire product.

The regiments formed the other ladder. The King's Musketeers and the Cardinal's Guard despised one another; the cavalry despised the infantry; everyone despised the Gascons; and the frontier regiments took whoever was left, including, on occasion, convicts. Commissions were bought with borrowed money, and rank had to be dressed for. Every summer the Minister of War called up brigades and sent them wherever France was bleeding that year, and gentlemen who had spent nine months perfecting the cut of a doublet spent three being shot at in a trench. Some volunteered in the off-seasons too — for pay, for plunder, for a line in the despatches — and some of their commanders were drunks, and some were fools, and the difference between a mention and a grave was very often one pip on a die.

And then there were the duels. A gentleman could not fight for just *any* reason — but rival suitors arriving at the same lady's doorstep, an insult to one's regiment, an indiscretion with another man's mistress: these were causes, judged valid by the community of one's peers, and a man who declined to answer them bled standing instead of blood. The duels themselves were fought not with luck but with *foresight*: each man secretly plotted his sequence of cuts, lunges, parries and rests, and the two designs collided like clockwork engines, blade ringing on blade exactly as often as one man had guessed the other's mind. It was chess at twenty-six degrees of blood. The only dice permitted were for the desperate act of *throwing* one's weapon — a gambler's stroke that will figure, unforgettably, later in this history.

## The chronicler's note

One more thing must be confessed. The Paris of this chronicle had gods, and the gods had names.

There was the **Narrator**, who decided what the colonels dreamed and what the ladies laughed at, who wrote the Gazette and carved the headstones; and there was a lesser, crueler spirit called the **Dice Golem**, who dwelt in a small brass box and answered every man's prayers with perfect indifference. The young men of Paris propitiated it, cursed it, flattered it, and blamed it — *"that incompetent Lieutenant D. Golém"* — and it killed them all the same. Where this chronicle pauses to note the table-talk of these gods and their worshippers, it does so in passages marked like this:

> 🎲 **At the table.** *Great Hearts, Noble Hearts* was played on a Discord server from August 2020 to June 2022, using the venerable rules of* En Garde! *(GDW, 1975; UK 4th edition). One real week made roughly one game month. The Narrator ran the world and also, gamely, ran player characters of his own into the same meat grinder as everyone else's — the fate of his first is recorded a few pages from now. A dozen players passed through Paris across five game years, and between them buried thirty-one characters. The asides will tell you what the dice were doing while the fiction burned.*

The reader now knows everything the young men knew when they stepped down from their coaches in March of 1624 — which is to say, nothing that would save them.

Let us meet them.
