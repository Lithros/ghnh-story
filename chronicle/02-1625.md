# Chapter II — 1625: The Year of the Republic

> *"A man does not live long in France. May as well make the best of it."*
> — Henri Toulouse

## I. Pies, elephants, and other affairs of honor (January–March)

The year began with a miracle: Jules Lavelle won a lady.

After a losing streak that had consumed ten months and the patience of half the mistresses in Paris — Octavie Vidal, Laurence Caillat, Héloïse Giteau, Raymonde Haillet, Sidonie Antier, and Suzanne LeBeau, who did not care for cheese — Jules presented Eugénie Landry with a diamond-encrusted brooch and was, at last, accepted. *"This day shall be remembered for all of history,"* he announced, and pranced in the street, and the *Gazette* covered the prancing.

![Gazette de France, January 1625](images/gazette/gazette-de-france_08_1625_Janvier.png)

The same season produced the most famous afternoon in the entire chronicle. Curtis Sinnoch, courting the exalted Octavie Vidal, had arranged for her pleasure a display of *two dozen pies*, each of a unique filling, laid out by privates of the Musketeers. Before the lady could taste the gooseberry, a rhythmic rumbling shook the street — and around the corner, in the colors of the Royal Marines, came Captain Alphonse Devereux *riding an elephant named Hannibal*, its trunk extended in practiced supplication.

What followed is best taken slowly, as the participants were forced to. The elephant demolished the pie table. The two friends — Curtis had personally sponsored Alphonse's entry into society — exchanged words that could not be withdrawn, Alphonse drawing his cutlass *from the elephant's back*, while the lady clapped her hands in delight and patted the animal. The Narrator's summary of the impasse deserves its immortality:

> *"The gentlemen stare one another down. The lady claps her hands in delight and pats the elephant. The elephant blinks... but will the gentlemen?"*

They did not blink; they dueled. Curtis, pointedly, chose the dagger — the commoner's blade, he explained, that the Devereux family had favored *before their elevation* — against Alphonse's cutlass, and on the second exchange took sixty-four points of measured cutlass across the shoulder. So Curtis threw the dagger. The sun, the record insists, burst from cover at precisely the wrong moment; the dirk went wide — and struck the elephant. Hannibal trumpeted in outrage and stampeded through what remained of the pies, and six privates were crushed to death trying to bring the beast under control.

*"Bah! The damned sun in my eyes,"* said Curtis, disarmed, wounded, and pie-less. *"As the pies are ruined, so is my bloodlust. The day is yours, Devereux."* Alphonse then rolled his courtship — needing, in the Narrator's phrase, a 2 "even with elephant desolation" — and won Octavie Vidal, abandoning his own Raymonde in the same gesture. Curtis's post-mortem, written to a friend, is the epitaph of the whole affair: *"Trodden pies, injured and enraged elephants, and the screams of dying peasantry soldiers — a horrifying, yet somehow appropriate metaphor for our brief stint here on Earth, eh?"* Chastened, he volunteered for the winter campaign to harden himself.

> 🎲 **At the table.** The elephant was not, strictly speaking, in the rules. The thrown-dagger miss was: Curtis needed a 3 or under and Dice Golem showed 5, and the Narrator simply followed the arc of the dagger to its logical destination. The same month the Narrator dropped two new house rules that reshaped the social game: unmarried mistresses reaching age thirty-five retire to a convent (*"It's treason then,"* was the players' reaction, as Suzanne LeBeau was sent packing), and a full **marriage rule** — propose after six months of courtship, resolve on one die: 4–6 accepted, 1 rejected *forever*; dowries, adultery penalties, and the ominous codicil *"Wives can produce heirs. Rules to come."* Minutes after it was posted, one player had his character roll a proposal **at the dice bot itself** — `/roll 1d6 #@Dice Golem, will you marry me?` — and the bot answered with a natural 6. *"WOW, HE DOES LOVE SOMEONE,"* cried the table. The suitor demurred ("DG is dozens of SL above me") and predicted the bot would kill him within the month for his presumption. It did.

## II. Blavet (February)

For the Huguenot admiral Soubise had shown his hand at the turn of the year: a navy assembled in secret, the coast of Brittany raided, the royal fleet caught at anchor. The Duke of Nevers's ships broke Soubise's line in the winter fighting, and the Duke of Vendôme was ordered to storm the fort the rebels held at Blavet harbor. The result was the amphibious catastrophe remembered simply as *Blavet*.

Vendôme's landing boats went in scrambled — the first roll of the operation was a 1, and everything descended from it. Leon Mandelbrot, the winemaker's son who had dueled his way to Laurence Caillat's heart and a captaincy in the 53rd, went into the surf with the first wave, eager beyond caution.

> *"Leon, in his eagerness, trips and hits his head on the landing plank. He drowns in the surf, unnoticed."*

The plank had claimed its second man. **Leon Mandelbrot, 1606–1625** — *he left his family behind*. In the grim bookkeeping that followed, his newly vacant captaincy passed to little Henri Toulouse, who had survived the same beach.

Nor was the plank finished. Guy de Cavoye — the earl's bastard who one month earlier had proposed marriage to the god of dice and been accepted — blew a kiss to "his betrothed, Dice Golem," chose recklessness a second time, and was announced to posterity in three words: *"Guy has exploded."* **Guy de Cavoye, 1607–1625** — *a real good guy*. His lady, the wealthy Héloïse Giteau, quit Paris entirely, retiring to the family château, "much to the horror and desolation of her Parisian friends."

There was glory at Blavet too, in the usual unequal shares. Colonel Besson's 53rd carved out a beachhead; the Royal Foot Guards, in the chronicle's most cherished sentence of military history, met the enemy's hand grenades by batting them back *"with their handy lacrosse sticks."* Curtis Sinnoch salvaged a stalemate from his corner of the fight, was Mentioned in Despatches, and, slipping into an abandoned boathouse as the fighting ebbed, found a chest containing a thousand crowns. Hercule Bonaparte, thrown into the sea with his horseless cuirassiers, organized the fighting retreat and came out of the water with a Mention of his own. But France withdrew by night; the Huguenots held Blavet; and Soubise began styling himself *Admiral of the Protestant Church*.

In Paris the Cardinal's response was total: no *Gazette* that month, by order — and a bounty of **ten thousand crowns** on the head of the Duke of Soubise.

![The Duke of Soubise](images/gazette/gazette-de-france_09_800px-Benjamin_de_Rohan_Duc_de_Frontenay_Baron_de_Soubise.jpg)

> 🎲 **At the table.** February 1625 (October 2020, at the table) was the weekend the founding generation broke. Two players rerolled within ninety minutes of one another. The one-year census taken shortly after found only four of the original March 1624 characters alive — Curtis, Pierre, Pain, Alphonse, "plus Dice Golem, that sour bastard." One player, asked how he was coping with his second death, noted he had "only rolled three characters, that's fewer than one a season."

## III. New blood, old money (March–April)

The replacements were, if anything, stranger than the men they replaced.

In place of Guy came **Lucien Vaillancourt**, a gentleman-bastard duelist who went nowhere without an entourage of trained doves and was observed, before his first bout, to *dual-wield pigeons*. His first week in Paris he was pulled into a duel with Curtis Sinnoch, who released his own falcon Archimedes to snatch a dove out of the air *pour l'ambiance*, then drove his sabre down through Lucien's shoulder "as though he were astride his charger" — a sickening crunch, splinters of clavicle spattering the onlookers. Lucien landed one desperate dove-guided lunge and surrendered on the spot, to Curtis's magnificent contempt: *"Pathetic. You slash a man, then immediately surrender. You made the right choice — leave with your tail between your legs, while you still stand."*

In place of Leon came **Jean Rochant**, who presented himself at the Frog & Peach with a mission statement: *"My father was a member of this club for years before he grew sick and lost his fortune. I have joined in the hopes that I may rebuild it!"* Paris would give Jean Rochant a fortune's worth of grievance instead.

The month's real contest was fought over the widow **Félice Brugière** — SL 13, thirty-two years old, influential enough (it was whispered) to have once declined the King, and newly eligible because her husband had just been executed for high treason. (Asked whether a mourning period applied, society concluded that mourning a traitor would be *highly suspect*.) Lucien Vaillancourt — he of the doves, whose blood was still drying on her doorstep from his welcome-to-Paris thrashing — greeted her with a flock of pigeons trained to spell her name in the sky; Curtis Sinnoch's falcon killed one of them mid-formation. *"Some sport, then, messieurs?"* smiled the widow. When the feathers settled, Curtis had won her — and cast off Adélaïde Blaise to do it. That night, Adélaïde received an unsigned note that historians of the affair consider the most chilling document of the year:

> *"One man for another. —F"*

The widow, it seemed, had orchestrated the entire courtship as a chess move. The Narrator himself confessed that "one cannot escape the feeling that this was precisely the outcome she intended."

April belonged to justice, or something wearing its coat. The murders had never stopped, not really — the *Gazette* called him the man "who leaves naught but Pain in his wake," and Paris had learned to read the capitalization. Curtis Sinnoch and Pierre de Tête, who had quietly agreed at Hunter's to pursue "private research on the matter," cut a deal with the Commissioner of Public Safety; the visit of the grieving Devereux family guaranteed a trial; and **Pain Loafmann** — Dutch banker, baguette enthusiast, gambling fiend, and (per the prosecution) the Stale Killer of the Rue Saint-Jacques — stood before the court. He mounted a half-hearted defense, half of it in Dutch. Guilty. One year's service in the Frontier Regiments, the sentence of a state that saw no reason a murderer should not murder usefully. He answered in haiku:

> *If God sees me back / You judge shall taste shaming dough / Winter seasonal!*

> 🎲 **At the table.** The GM revealed the mechanism in the table-talk channel that same week, in one line of Hamilton: *"An agreement was made... and no one else was in the room where it happened."* Two players had reported Pain to the authorities — the standing meta-rule being that **no player could ever be arrested without another player causing it**. The verdict itself was resolved on an open 2d6 in the channel. *"Wow, a roll of the dice on a Court decision,"* marveled one player. *"This game really is like real life."* And the epitaph-writers were already sharpening their pens: *"Watch him gain honor in combat."* Reader: within four months, the convict was Mentioned in Despatches — the Narrator annotating the honor **"Sentence: 25% served. Convict: 0% rehabilitated."**

## IV. Nantes falls; a knight is made (April–May)

Soubise's rebels, flush from Blavet, marched south to link arms with restive La Rochelle, and the frontier regiments were fed into Nantes to hold them at three-to-one odds. Their commander, Colonel Christian Solé, was midway through an inspirational address when he saw the Huguenot charge begin, spurred his horse *away* from the battlefield, and left his adjutant holding the speech. The men held anyway, briefly. Curtis Sinnoch's battalion of King's Musketeers bought the line time; he came away with his second Mention in Despatches, and the King — who had "caught wind of his talents" — reached for the decree that would have made him a knight, "but is distracted by his mistress. Maybe next time..."

The knighthood went instead to the man whose whole life had been arranged to intercept it. Hercule Bonaparte's cuirassiers ran roughshod over the Protestant militia; his consolation Mention came with the King's eye attached, and this time the die came up the natural 6 the honor demanded. **Sir Hercule Bonaparte** — the merchant's son with the thirty-point constitution, the plank-orphan, the blackmailer of parks and engineer of ladies' worst days — was knighted before the court, his social standing catapulted overnight into the nobility he had priced to the sou.

> *"As the King presents the new knight to the Court, the exceptionally observant note a slight upward curl at the corners of his mouth."*

Nantes fell anyway. Colonel Solé was executed for his poltroonery, *pour encourager les autres*. And the Minister of War, taking stock of a war going badly everywhere, rolled up the great levy of the summer: every brigade in France save one, which meant, for the first time, *every man in this chronicle at once* — even the convict Loafmann, marching with the Frontier Regiments at the King's insistence and his own considerable amusement.

> *"WE ALL GET TO DIE!"* — Jean Rochant, upon reading the mobilization order

## V. The forest and the Republic (June–August)

What France attempted in the summer of 1625 was a sweep: the Field Army, under the inexplicably reinstated General Gilles Barbier, driving through the heavily forested Atlantic coast to root out the Protestant militias — while borrowed English and Dutch ships under Vice-Admiral Van Dorp prowled the sea lanes, Richelieu having mortgaged concessions to both nations to replace the navy France no longer had.

The forest ate the army.

The chronicle of those three months is a catalogue of institutional suicide narrated with terrible cheerfulness. The Guards Brigade, ordered by its glory-starved brigadier to fire blind into the trees "to frighten the rebels into surrender," slaughtered the retreating men of its own Frontier Division — whose screams drew in the Dragoon Brigade, which crushed the Guards in turn: a three-way French-on-French melee in the middle of an enemy forest. An unseen cannon emplacement blew the Horse Guards commander's mount out from under him mid-gallop. The Frontier Division's convict commander, asked by the Frontier Regiment's own colonel how best to advance, is recorded to have replied: *"Certainly not by joining the Frontier Regiment, sir."* Another colonel, deciding "there are simply too many ladies left to woo," ordered his men to play dead — "and some listen," the record notes, "perhaps even" the convict Loafmann.

Through this abattoir the player characters threaded with the mixed fortunes of the truly damned. Hercule rode his squadron into a tripwire-and-spear trap and saved most of it by intuition. Rochant survived. Lucien pocketed a five-hundred-crown share of captured war funds. Pain earned his Mention. And Pierre de Tête — the founding cohort's great sword, the hero of the Croquant war, Brigade Major, Mentioned in Despatches while the rest of his generation was learning to bleed — found himself, in the words of the record, "sabre-deep in a Royal Footman, and not in the fun way," as friendly formations collided in the smoke. He flourished his sabre, spraying his comrades with gore, and shouted his last words:

> *"No more quill pen pushing for me!"*

The Colonel of the Royal Foot Guards roared *"FOR FRANCE!"* and put a rapier through his eye. **Pierre de Tête, 1605–1625** — *he had a real eye for the blade.* His player's verdict on the killer: "well, he wasn't entirely wrong."

The butcher's bill among the general staff ran to two lieutenant generals, two brigadiers, three colonels, and the officer corps of a dozen regiments. *"The Summer Campaign is a disaster,"* the Narrator pronounced. *"Thousands of men are slaughtered in the woods outside Nantes."* And then the sentence that broke the war open:

> **"With the vast Protestant army at its gates, La Rochelle's city council votes to join Duke Soubise. August 8th marks the formation of the French Protestant Republic, with Soubise anointed as Lord Protector."**

France was now two countries, and the western one had better ports.

> 🎲 **At the table.** *"God, what a bloodbath,"* said the GM, surveying his own handiwork — though a player noted survival had run near ninety percent that summer, the dice sparing nearly everyone the narrative did not. Asked how the campaign went, the players adopted the official verdict as a catchphrase: **"Inconclusive."** The summer also produced the fastest funeral pun on record. Learning that Pierre's slayer had cried "For France," his player — burying her first character, with six more ahead of her — shrugged: *"Serves me right for getting in a fight with my own army."*

## VI. The cane bonus (September)

The armies limped home to a Paris that immediately resumed its real war: the war for position.

Into it stepped a new man — **Amand Baschet**, second son of a knight, very wealthy, a painter by passion, who introduced himself to society by posing as Adélaïde Blaise's new watercolors tutor, transforming her drawing room into a Neptunian stage set, and presenting her a pink Grecian gown and a sea-star crown so that he might paint her as Aphrodite. The lady abandoned Jean Rochant — then four months at the front, faithfully writing — and embraced the dashing tutor. Rochant learned of it by letter, in camp, alongside the detail that his rival bore "a name that seemed to ring a distant bell, but now was one committed to memory."

The autumn's grandest farce, however, played out in the orderly room of the Royal Foot Guards, where a single Majority stood open and two of the most decorated men in France arrived to claim it. Sir Hercule Bonaparte was rejected on the merits — until his lover Alida's discreet letter reached the acting commander, who, tucking away the perfumed envelope, announced that he had forgotten to apply *the cane bonus*: *"Yes. The cane adds dignity. The Royal Foot Guards... prize... dignity."* Curtis Sinnoch answered with the Sinnoch name; an influence-bidding war erupted, favours burned on both sides; Jules Lavelle threw his weight publicly behind the knight — *"I may be a mere Captain in this regiment. But I have experience in leadership"* — and the tie fell to Hercule.

Then the aftermath, in strict order: the Sinnoch family wrote to the Minister of War; the acting commander was dismissed to the Frontier Regiments for mishandling the process; Curtis was handed a junior Majority anyway by way of apology; and the King, reviewing the whole affair, ruled that the Royal Foot Guards must be commanded by a man with a title in his own right — which no Sinnoch yet held. Thus did **Sir Hercule Bonaparte, Acting Commander of the Royal Foot Guards**, complete the most improbable ascent in the chronicle: from a plank's punchline to command of the King's own infantry in fourteen months.

Curtis, on his way out, challenged Jules to a duel — *"I'll have an explanation, or I'll have blood... You couldn't lead a wet mop out of a closet!"* — and got the blood: a proper intra-regimental affair before the assembled Guards, Jules asking that if he died, Lady Landry might visit his grave every full moon. Curtis cut his friend's thigh to the quick; Jules went down to one knee, using his sword as a cane (the regiment, presumably, awarding dignity points), and yielded. Within the week Curtis invited him to Bothwell's by way of apology, and Jules's rage — the record is frank — "evaporates in an instant."

> 🎲 **At the table.** The GM admitted afterward that he had missed one of Curtis's influence submissions — there should never have been a tie at all. The duel that resolved the scandal was fought explicitly without murderous intent, Jules noting with relief that this opponent, unlike certain persons who had once *paid a peasant to throw daggers at him*, was "not going to try to MURDER me." The spectators' hashtag war during the slow build-up — #WhenWillThereBeBlood, #StillWeekOne, #IveGotMyActionsReady, #IDont — remains the finest piece of seventeenth-century social media on record.

## VII. Île de Ré, and the plank ascendant (October–December)

The counterstroke came in the autumn. With Dutch and English warships breaking the Protestant navy at sea, the Frontier Regiments landed on the **Île de Ré** to reduce Soubise's island fortress — the war's first true siege, commanded by Colonel Jean-Pascal Boucher, "a man of uncommon tactical brilliance who happened to tell an off-color joke about the Queen at the wrong time."

Boucher's loose formations reached the walls without a single enemy-inflicted casualty. Lieutenant-Colonel Bonaparte, commanding the second prong of the trident, delivered to his men an elaborate oration on the *golden bridge* — always leave a desperate enemy a road to flee down, that his morale may crumble at no cost to yourselves — and rolled a 2: the speech proved "simply too engrossing," and by the time he finished and his men caught up, the siege was mostly over. Clemens Charron, the peasant ox, was promoted captain in the storm and emerged from the Duke's private apartments with the tea service of Soubise himself, valued at fourteen hundred crowns: *"I am going to drink... so much tea."* The Duke was taken alive. The Cardinal's Guard officers who celebrated by drinking the Duke's abandoned wine were found dead the next morning; Soubise had poisoned his own cellar before surrendering. Even in captivity, the Lord Protector took tricks.

And yet the siege's most famous casualty was inflicted by no enemy at all. In an assault without a single enemy-inflicted loss, Lucien Vaillancourt — reckless to the last, emboldened by the ease of the advance — went up the fort's exterior wall to show off, chose for a handhold a plank that proved rotten, and fell to his death before the eyes of two armies. **Lucien Vaillancourt, 1608–1625** — *we thought he would never stop climbing.* Six plank emoji attended the announcement; the gag was now a god. His last written testament survives: *"I am a bastard, a corpse, but not a coward — for what that's worth."*

The volunteer season ground on into November, and the Narrator's monthly bulletin — *"November passes without incident"* — was immediately corrected by the bereaved: *"Tell that to Lucien and Pain."* For the convict Loafmann had also thrown his last boxcars at the front, dying half a sentence from freedom and none of it repented. **Pain Loafmann, 1608–1625** — *wrongly j'accused*, says the stone, and the chronicle takes no position on the adverb, except to note that the killings stopped.

Paris closed the year in high style regardless. Hercule came out of the autumn a brevet **Colonel of the Royal Foot Guards** — his social level not yet quite equal to the full dignity — having computed openly, back in the season of his knighthood, that with the title, the appointment, and the despatches he "could just spend the entire month in my mistress's bedchamber and gain a Social Level" (*"the most gentlemanly way to rank up,"* Jules had agreed), and having added, quietly, the sentence that hangs over the rest of his story: *"I am beginning to fear my success is going to get me killed."* Jean Rochant, robbed of Adélaïde, won the great Laurence Caillat on the strength of — the Narrator's ruling — "the fiery look in your eyes, the hatred with which you speak the name *Baschet*"; and then, in the season's crowning scandal, marched to Amand Baschet's door and challenged him outright, while Paris savored the colonial coinage that attached itself to the painter forever: *Mr. Steal Yo' Girl*. Baschet loftily offered a free portraiture session — *"A well-respected son is the jewel of a mother and father's eye"* — unaware that he was addressing an orphan.

> *"YOU STEAL MY MISTRESS AND NOW YOU THROW MY LACK OF PARENTS IN MY FACE?"* — Jean Rochant, accepting nothing

The duel was set for the new year. At Bothwell's — three floors of discretion on the Seine, where the King himself was said to appear — Curtis Sinnoch hosted Jules Lavelle for a Christmas of firsts, including Jules's first encounter with a novel colonial beverage: *"What is this drink they're serving? 'Chocolate?' Is it healthy?"*

It was the last peaceful December the chronicle records for a long time.
