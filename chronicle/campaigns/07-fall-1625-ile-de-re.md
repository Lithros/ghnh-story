# Fall 1625 — Île de Ré: Not a Single (Enemy-Inflicted) Casualty

*September – November 1625 · resolved at the table 2–3 December 2020 · Mission: Siege · Theater: Île de Ré, off the Atlantic coast of France*

---

## The situation

Summer had ended in catastrophe: the woods outside Nantes had swallowed thousands of the King's men, La Rochelle had gone over to the rebels, and the Duke of Soubise had crowned himself Lord Protector of a brand-new French Protestant Republic. The fall campaign would be France's answer. On the evening of 2 December the Narrator set the board:

**Narrator** — "The Protestant Navy, having taken the coast, quickly moved to establish control over strategic islands it could use as ports of refuge from royal reinforcements. One island in particular, Île de Ré, has become a central gathering point from which the Huguenots launch raids on unsuspecting French towns."

**Narrator** — "The Dutch and English have once again lent their warships to the cause. the Duke of Montmorency requests the Frontier Regiments to aid in capturing Ré."

> 🎲 Narrator rolls 1d6 — **1** *(the table winced)*

**Narrator** — "A fierce naval battle ensues. Although several Dutch ships are lost with all hands, the Protestant Navy is finally crushed. Soubise retreats to the fortress on the island's northwest point. English ships deliver the Frontier Regiments to the island's unprotected southern beaches and wish them Godspeed."

**Narrator** — "The Frontier Regiments will undertake a Siege."

*The Royal Foot Guards were not even supposed to be there — they had volunteered themselves. Two weeks earlier, in the off-season channel, their acting commander had read the newspapers and made a decision:*

**Sir Hercule Bonaparte** — "Excellent, Capt. @Jules Lavelle. Having read correspondence from the front, I am underwhelmed by our progress. After these last few campaigns, France is in need of a grand victory to ensure neighboring nations do not begin to question our military might. Now is not the time for its greatest fighting force to be sitting on the side lines. Please have the appropriate dispatches prepared and sent, advising the men to indulge themselves this October, for November we march to the aid of our countrymen."

**Jules Lavelle** — "Yes, sir!!"

**Sir Hercule Bonaparte** — "@Curtis Sinnoch @Jules Lavelle and the rest of the Royal Foot Guard: I trust you all took advantage of your respite in October. Now let us go and remind France and the world that it has one of the greatest fighting forces on the planet."

**Narrator** — "Cols. Boucher and Devereux, alongside Lt. Col. Bonaparte, agree to arrange their forces into a trident, with Boucher leading the central thrust and the Royal Regiments on the flanks."

## The record

*The central thrust went first.*

**Narrator** — "The island offers poor cover for advancing on a fortified position. Boucher orders his battalions into loose formations, to diminish the effectiveness of enemy batteries."

> 🎲 Narrator rolls 1d6 — **6**

**Narrator** — "The tactic works even better than expected. The Frontier Regiment attains the walls of the fort without suffering a single casualty, as the panicking defenders have failed to properly calibrate their cannons."

**Narrator** — "The Frontier Regiments have succeeded in their portion of the Siege, with a result of 1."

*A perfect assault — a decisive victory without one man lost to the enemy. The men of the central thrust settled their personal fates on the spot (see The reckoning below, where that phrase "not a single casualty" gets its asterisk). Then the left flank stirred:*

**Narrator** — "@WillWorker, please roll for your battle result." — "Your forces are prepared to advance on the flank."

**Sir Hercule Bonaparte** — "Alright men, the enemy is cornered, so now is the time for patience. There is no need for unnecessary recklessness, we control the tempo, not them. And while I know many of you will wish this as an opportunity for vengeance, when you encounter cowards you are to show them mercy and take them prisoner. Do not give our quarry reason to believe they must fight in desperation to the last. Show them a golden bridge on which they may have some hope of seeing tomorrow and their morale shall crumble with barely any effort on our part."

> 🎲 Hercule Bonaparte rolls 1d6 — **2**

**Narrator** — "Your men follow your words, occasionally glancing over at the center and right flank which have already begun to advance. Yet your speech is simply too engrossing. By the time you finish and your men catch up, the siege is mostly over. Your result is Inconclusive, a 4."

*On the right flank, the Royal Marines waited until evening for their moment. Jean Rochant grew impatient:*

**Jean Rochant** — "@Anonymouse, We wish to bring our own glory to France! When do we enter the fray!?"

**Col. Alphonse Devereux** — "Onward, men! For France!"

> 🎲 Alphonse Devereux rolls 1d6 — **4**

**Narrator** — "The Royal Marines were expecting an amphibious assault, not one made parallel to the shore. They charge forward, but slip and fall over the uneven sandy expanse that covers their flank, making little progress. This is an Inconclusive result of 4."

*And that was the whole siege: the center took the walls flawlessly while one flank listened to a speech and the other fell over in the sand. Soubise's fortress fell, and the Duke himself was taken — the capture confirmed in the Narrator's own words a day later ("the Duke had the wine poisoned before his capture") and again at the opening of the winter campaign: "in the wake of the capture of the Duke of Soubise, the last remaining Protestant strongholds have been captured and demilitarized."*

## Aftermath

*On 3 December the Narrator rolled the winter volunteer lottery, regiment by regiment — CG 3, KM 4, DG 3, QOC 4, ALC 5, CPC 1, PM 4:*

**Narrator** — "no regiments are going. I'll roll the battalions separately and let you know" — "Battalions volunteering: Cardinal's Guard, 1st / Queen's Own Carabiniers, 3rd / Archduke Leopold's Cuirassiers, 2nd (no players affected)"

**Jean Rochant** — "Phew"

*The Narrator had already teased the table about it the night before: "unless you want to volunteer for winter.... but you might make poor Jean sad." Alphonse's answer: "Nope. Not going to volunteer for Winter." Even so, on 8 December the campaign channel carried one more announcement — "@harry has volunteered for the Frontier Regiment" — Captain Charron, tea service in hand, heading back toward the guns of his own free will.*

*The same afternoon Lucien died, his player's next man was already strolling the streets of Paris:*

**Marius Thibodeaux** — "I have just arrived in Paris and already feel a bout of ennui overtaking me. What do you people do for fun here?" *(reactions: plank ×3)*

*And on the 7th, the heroes came home from Ré to discover what Paris had been doing while they bled — Amand Baschet had spent the fall courting Jean's mistress:*

**Jean Rochant** — "I return from the front, after honorably defending France under the command of @Anonymouse only to discovery that my dearest Adelaide Blaise has been stolen away by the dishonorable snake @DancingHorse, who took advantage of my service to king and country to take advantage and become, as the colloquialism in the colonies goes 'Mr. Steal Yo' Girl.' As a result, I issue a December challenge to @DancingHorse, while not for Mme. Blaise's hand, but for his dishonorable actions."

**Jean Rochant** — "And as you will note, I do not blame Adelaide, but rather that coward @DancingHorse, who sits in Paris while the rest of us stumble on the beaches and fight for country!"

**Jules Lavelle** — "He is a cavalryman; he was in need of a sturdy mount."

*That duel is winter's story.*
