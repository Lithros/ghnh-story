# Winter 1625–26 — No Quarter on the Sèvre Niortaise

*December 1625 – February 1626 · resolved at the table 30 December 2020 · Mission: Field Operations · Theater: the West Coast of France — the Sèvre Niortaise, Poitou*

---

## The situation

The fall had broken the rebellion's back: the Duke of Soubise taken at Île de Ré, his fleet scattered, his strongholds falling one by one. What remained was not a war but a hunt. On the night of 30 December the Narrator opened the ledger on **The Winter Campaign**:

**Narrator** — "Aided by the Dutch and English ships, in the wake of the capture of the Duke of Soubise, the last remaining Protestant strongholds have been captured and demilitarized. All that remains is to scour the countryside for scattered pockets of traitor militia."

> 🎲 Narrator rolls 1d6 for the mission — **6**

**Narrator** — "As such, all regiments will be performing Field Operations, hunting down the traitors wherever they may be found along the West Coast."

*The decision to march had been taken weeks earlier, in the Royal Foot Guards' mess, where the regiment's brevet Colonel put the question to his officers:*

**Hercule Bonaparte** — "While I am loathe to leave the comforts of Paris to campaign in the winter, three battalions is a paltry amount to be fielding. What say you gentlemen? With so few on the field, there would be plenty of glory for us. Shall we enjoy our Christmas now and greet the New Year showing France the true grit of its Royal Guard."

**Jules Lavelle** — "Sir, wherever you go, I shall follow. To glory!!"

**Curtis Sinnoch** — "Personally, I am in favor of staying in Paris for both Dec and Jan - One of the best ways to stay warm is the company of our fine wines and finer ladies! Let the rebels squander their meager rations and hope, and when they are most stretched thin, we ride in Feb and crush them like the insects they are!"

*Christmas was duly enjoyed, and by mid-January the regiment was pawing at the ground:*

**Curtis Sinnoch** — "Ah, my fellows - it was a lovely Christmas and New Year spent at home, but I feel like, well rested, it is time for us to march to the front! What say you, commander? What shall your orders be?"

**Jules Lavelle** — "I stand ready as always to lay my life on the line for France!"

**Hercule Bonaparte** — "I believe I made my intentions clear. We shall not delay France's glory a moment longer."

**Curtis Sinnoch** — "Thank you for the confirmation - this February, we shall ride and bring a swift victory for France!"

## The record

### The Royal Foot Guards at the river

**Narrator** — "Independently ranging, the Royal Foot Guards happen upon a contingent of Protestant troops waiting to ferry across the Sèvre Niortaise. Once across, the traitors are sure to burn the boats, eliminating any chance of capture. WillWorker, what happens next is up to you."

**Hercule Bonaparte** — "Jules Lavelle — Prepare and send dispatches for a full assault on their flank opposite the Frontier Regiment. Tell the men to offer no quarter and an make examples of those who do not flee in terror. We shall drive them from the shoreline away from their boats in the waiting arms of capture by the Frontier Regiment. And, if any do elude us, the memory of what happens to traitors will be forever etched into their minds, such that they will never raise a hand against France again. Task and position Curtis Sinnoch with the important duty of securing any path back to their boats once we have pushed them away from the shore. Last thing we want is some thinking they can double back."

**Jules Lavelle** — "Yes, sir! On the nonce!"

> 🎲 Hercule Bonaparte rolls 1d6 for his battle result — **5**

**Narrator** — "The enemy stands utterly unprepared to do battle. The first volley launched from ambush rips through the men already on boats, leaving their broken bodies strewn across the damaged rivercraft. The rest of the enemy force is thrown into disarray, unable to organize itself to clear the boats and make haste, nor to mount any kind of meaningful counterstrike. A few poltroons swim for it, avoiding the enemy's complete annihilation, but nothing of any significance remains intact on the field. Your battle result is a 2."

**Hercule Bonaparte** — "Excellent work, men!"

*The Guards' personal reckonings were tallied first (see below); then the Narrator turned to the other column:*

**Narrator** — "Attempting to maneuver his volunteers in concert with the expertly-drilled Royal Foot Guards, Col. Jean-Yves De la Croix orders a series of overlapping formations to spread out across the countryside."

**Narrator** — "Only small pockets of resistance are encountered, and most of the unwilling volunteers decline to seriously engage them. Where they do fight, it goes poorly for the Frontier troops. The battle result is a 4, Inconclusive."

### Honors and the ledger

The no-quarter ambush on the Sèvre Niortaise made the Royal Foot Guards' winter. **Hercule Bonaparte** came home mentioned in despatches a fourth time (+6 SP for three months), promoted to **full Colonel** of the Royal Foot Guards, and 400 crowns richer from the Protestant payroll — though the King declined to add a title to the pile. **Curtis Sinnoch** was mentioned a fifth time, promoted to **Lieutenant Colonel**, released from the Brigade-General's staff, and intercepted the bulk of the traitors' payroll on the road: 700 crowns. **Jules Lavelle** took a **brevet Majority** and 500 crowns, and traded the adjunct's desk for battalion command. In the Frontier Regiment, **Clemens Charron** marched home unpaid, unmentioned, and alive — by his own accounting, good news. **Alphonse Devereux** did not march home at all.
