# Winter 1624–25 — The Storming of Blavet

*December 1624 – February 1625 · resolved at the table 17–18 October 2020 · Mission: Siege (amphibious assault) · Theater: Blavet harbor, Brittany*

---

## The situation

The Huguenot rebellion had put a fleet to sea, and the winter's work began on the water. When the mission die came up **1** — "This is a Siege," the Narrator announced — he set the scene:

**Narrator** — "After a decisive naval engagement between the Royal Fleet commanded by the Duke of Nevers and the Huguenot Navy under the Duke of Soubise, the rebels found their backs broken by the brilliant maneuvers of Nevers, who said 'Never!' to the chances of Huguenot independence."

**Narrator** — "The Huguenots retreat into the fort in Blavet harbor. The Duke of Vendôme is assigned to conduct an amphibious landing and take the fort."

*Word of the deployment had gone out weeks earlier, back in November. The Royal Foot Guards and the 53rd Fusiliers volunteered on 6s, and the men marked for the boats took the news each in his own key:*

**Guy de Cavoye** — "At least I shall be paid my regiment's salary this time!"

**Henri Toulouse** — "Well, why not. My luck has been total shite, so my expectations can only be met, not disappointed."

**Leon Mandelbrot** — "We'll be fine Henri! … I'll be helping in the direction from atop my horse! … Oh wait, revieweing the rosters, you're not in my company! … Best of luck then!"

**Henri Toulouse** — "One small mercy, I suppose."

**Leon Mandelbrot** — "In fairness, being in my company did not help poor Phillipe"

## The order of battle

*In the January before the sailing, two gentlemen bought their way to field rank — Curtis Sinnoch purchasing a majority in the King's Musketeers ("Congratulations to the new junior Major in the King's Musketeers!" — the Narrator), and Hercule Bonaparte at last winning entry to the Crown Prince Cuirassiers:*

**Hercule Bonaparte** — "At last, I can prove my mettle."

**Guy de Cavoye** — "We have some Major spenders here!"

*Both new majors volunteered their own battalions for the winter campaign. On the eve of departure the streets emptied of soldiers:*

**Guy de Cavoye** — "I once again feel the love of France burning in my loins!"

**Jules Lavelle** — "Which way to the camp followers, then, sir?"

**Hercule Bonaparte** — "Hercule sees the stream of the young men of France heading out to campaign. The question of Jules Lavelle regarding his service to France echoing in his ear. The fame that such service could garner echoing even louder. 'I have my commission now,' he mutters to himself, 'and one can only get so far on carousing and politicking... no sense leaving all the glory to these lot.' He writes two letters. The first, instructions to his staff regarding upkeep of the estate while he is away. The second, a brief note to Alida explaining his absence and that he will keep the calm understanding of her company within his heart. A moment of regretful emotion crosses his face before returning to its normal steel-eyed gaze. He dons his uniform, gives himself a steady once over in the full length mirror, and heads out to war."

**Jules Lavelle** — "Knee-deep in prostitutes, Jules momentarily realizes he forgot to tell Eugenie where he'd be this month. Ah, well. C'est la guerre, c'est l'amour."

**Guy de Cavoye** — "It feels good to have so many of my countrymen join me in this campaign!"

*Seven players answered the muster call — but the staff officers were promptly sent back to their desks:*

**Narrator** — "Correction. Jules Lavelle is serving as brigade major and not allowed to volunteer. His month is wasted" — "Same goes for Abelard Soucy [Pierre de Tête]."

**Narrator** — "Ghôst Touchard [Guy de Cavoye] campaigns with the Royal Foot Guards. Curtis Sinnoch has volunteered his battalion, operating under the Frontier Regiment. Blastagarded [Hercule Bonaparte] has volunteered his battalion, operating under the Frontier Regiment. Jean Renault [Leon Mandelbrot] and laiv.wire [Henri Toulouse] campaign with the 53rd Fusiliers."

**Narrator** — "You gain +1 MA for salvaging a stalemate from the jaws of disaster."

*Then the fusiliers:*

**Narrator** — "Sébastien Besson, Colonel of the 53rd Fusiliers, has seen many battles in his time. He manages to keep his boats together and land on the beach as one regiment."

> 🎲 Narrator rolls 1d6 for the 53rd — **5**

**Narrator** — "Col. Besson takes charge of the landing, and his regiment steps out onto the beach before the imposing fortress in good battle order. The 1st Battalion provides cover while the 2nd & 3rd advance up the beach. They harass the flanks of the enemy sorties, and fully establish a beachhead!"

*Here Captain Leon Mandelbrot's and Subaltern Henri Toulouse's fates were decided — see The reckoning below. With the beachhead won, the reserve was committed:*

**Narrator** — "With the establishment of a beachhead, Col. Bouchard of the Royal Foot Guards orders his men to charge forward to reinforce the beleaguered 53rd."

> 🎲 Narrator rolls 1d6 for the Royal Foot Guards — **6**

**Narrator** — "The enemy attempts to pelt the 53rd with hand grenades, but the Royal Foot Guards toss them right back with their handy lacrosse sticks."

**Narrator** — "A successful storming!"

*It was in this storm of returned grenades that Subaltern Guy de Cavoye met his end — see below. Last of all came the Crown Prince Cuirassiers, horsemen put into rowboats:*

> 🎲 Hercule Bonaparte rolls 1d6 — **1**

**Blastagarded (player)** — "oof"

**Narrator** — "Hercule's battalion, without horses, is utterly unprepared for the intensity of fighting amphibiously. They suffer horrible losses as they find themselves repelled back into the sea."

*And when the last man's dice had fallen, the Narrator pronounced the verdict of the day:*

**Narrator** — "The fighting outside the fortress is madness. Men pour from boats and the fort, scream, and die. Great struggles are waged over bare feet of land between the sea and fortress walls. Although the armies of France are valiant, they are unable to make real progress in seizing the fortress. They hold position throughout the day, but as night falls, each battalion slips back to its boats and retreats from the harbor. The Huguenots are victorious.

Duke Soubise names himself 'Admiral of the Protestant Church.'"

### Honors, casualties, and the ledger

Two player characters died in the surf and smoke at Blavet — **Leon Mandelbrot**, drowned under his own landing plank, and **Guy de Cavoye**, exploded by a grenade his comrades failed to lacrosse away. **Henri Toulouse** came home a Captain (stepping, in all likelihood, into his dead comrade's slot); **Curtis Sinnoch** was mentioned in despatches (+1 SP per month for life) and 1,000 crowns richer for his boathouse discovery; **Hercule Bonaparte** was mentioned in despatches after his fighting retreat.

The NPC officers fared little better. In the Royal Foot Guards the Lieutenant-Colonel and three captains were killed (rolls of 9, 8, 9, and 11 against their death numbers); Curtis's own battalion of the King's Musketeers lost no officers; the Crown Prince Cuirassiers lost a captain —

**Narrator** — "Couldn't lead him out of that one, Blastagarded...."

**Blastagarded (player)** — "That man was an idiot. If I could, I would have prompted his horse to his position."

— and the 53rd Fusiliers lost their Lieutenant-Colonel, a Major, and Captain D. Except:

**Narrator** — "oh never mind … Cap D was Leon"

**laiv.wire (player)** — "Double death"

**Jean Renault (player)** — "NOOOOOOO … Wait … Does that mean I come back to life?"

**Narrator** — "you come back to life. your lungs are still full of water. you drown again immediately"

**Jean Renault (player)** — "Worth"

In the promotion rolls that followed, a Major and a Captain of the Royal Foot Guards and a Captain of the 53rd Fusiliers were advanced. Then came the spring musters: the Dragoon Guards were volunteered ("As Brigade Major, Jules is exempted from service," the Narrator noted), and both Curtis Sinnoch and Hercule Bonaparte chose to lead their battalions out yet again — toward Nantes, and the Huguenot army now marching in triumph from Blavet.

### Aftermath in Paris (March 1625)

*The defeat was too shameful to print:*

**Narrator** *(in the Gazette de France)* — "By Order of the Cardinal there is to be no Gazette published this month. A bounty of 10,000 crowns has been offered for the head of the Duke of Soubise."

**Clemens Charron** — "Is there a bounty for any other part of the man?"

*In the streets, the survivors and the newly arrived took stock:*

**Lucien Vaillancourt** — "War is hell, boys. Those who come back come back as changed men. Those who do not come back come back as changed men."

**Jules Lavelle** — "You think that's hell? Try running errands for a geriatric brigadier-general! … I'd take the war any day...."

**Clemens Charron** — "It would seem that hell is, most accurately, others."

*And the man with the boathouse chest turned his plunder to hospitality:*

**Curtis Sinnoch** — "Fine Gentleman! To be back upon the streets, as the brutal chill of winter starts to leave, so does my heart once warm! Life! To Life! All men of good standing and intentions are invited to join me at Hunter's this month! The last of the month! The first round is on me!"
