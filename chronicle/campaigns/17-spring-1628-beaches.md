## The situation

**Narrator** — "The Spring Campaign"

**Narrator** — "As the siege of La Rochelle proceeds, the English show no signs of abandoning their attempts to relieve the city. Week after week, ships of all sizes attempt to slip through the blockade, and enough succeed that the city continues to maintain its resistance, despite all odds."

While the French military would normally be hard-pressed to resist such a concentration, even without English army support, the volunteers out of Paris have been unusually enthusiastic this season, and the Cardinal orders the hodgepodge of regiments deployed in force."

### Jean Shawinigan — Captain, under Col. LaRue — the temporal paradox

**Narrator** — "While we wait, @Jean Shawinigan, your rolls are Death 6, Mention 7, Promotion 6. No chance for loot."

*The player answered the ugly numbers with the fullest confession his time-traveling character ever made:*

**Jean Shawinigan** — "Jean knew going to war was a risk, but this wasn't the first conflict he'd been tasked with observing. And the potential benefit to securing a better and more comfortable existence in this barbaric hellhole he was stranded in seemed worth it.

However, as Jean found out, it is one thing to be a temporal observer of a war and another thing entirely to be a participant. As soon as he stepped onto the field of battle, the temporal database went berserk. With the butterfly effect wreaking its havoc and Jean witnessing Frenchman everywhere laying down their lives for a hard won fight, the probability map for the outcome as a costless victory for the French dropped from Reasonable Certainty to Flux.

All bets were now off. Both for his survival and that of the version of the future he came from. Records from the 1600s were sparse for details as to how events actually unfolded. And it could merely be that the historical records were wrong. The timeline may be fine and the bloodless accounts from this era were simply the result of a bias by those who wrote the ending.

But with the battle engaged, there was no time to determine if and how his presence could alter things so significantly. Survive first. Fix later.

'Alright men, follow my lead,' Jean shouted, 'Our charge will take us perilously close to the enemy before we dismount. In fact, I encourage you all to plow your horse into the enemy as you leap from the saddle.'

With that, Jean led the charge, hoping an overwhelming show of devastation would demoralize the enemy into leaving him be. As he closed in on the carnage, Jean used his enhanced strength and agility to leap from the saddle. While his horse trampled over two men, his landing coincided with his blade cutting down another Englishman who was too dumbstruck by the scene to react. The battle joined, all that was left was for the time traveler to hope fate would favor him."

### The butcher's bill and the aftermath

*Four player characters dead in a single resolution — the worst day of the entire war: Brigadier General Marius Thibodeaux, flattened under a galleon on a freak wave; Captain Jean Shawinigan, vaporized by his own future; Captain Anton Dechets, chest-shot by a booby-trapped smuggler's cannon; Private Pierre d'Amboise, beheaded by the same ball three hundred meters on. The Frontier Regiment was shattered by the Reavers, its rear ranks with their throats slit in the surf. Against that: a Brevet Brigadier-Generalcy, a mention, and 200 crowns of Spanish doubloons for Marquis Sinnoch; 700 skimmed crowns for Lt. Col. Lavelle; a 100-crown roadside ring for Lt. Col. Renault; and a subaltern's commission and 200 crowns for Slade Imbee. Four tombstones went up in the cemetery channel on the morning of 15 December, one after another between 12:35 and 12:39.*
