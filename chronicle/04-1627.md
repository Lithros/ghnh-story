# Chapter IV — 1627: The Year of the English

> *"War is hell, boys. Those who come back come back as changed men. Those who do not come back come back as changed men."*
> — the player behind Sir Marius Thibodeaux, in an earlier life (winter 1625)

## I. The winter of the sea-bass slap (January–February)

The year opened with a courtship conducted like a siege. Guerinet Durand — the wealthy merchant's son who had replaced the unlucky Baschet line — had spent months writing anonymous letters to Armadine Demachy until her keepsake box was "full to busting," and crowned the campaign at Christmas with the full Twelve Days in physical gifts, down to five golden rings scavenged from the previous summer's battlefield ("It feels symbolic to him"). He revealed himself with a kiss beneath the mistletoe, waving away the drummers and the pipers, and the lady's heart was "fully and totally swept away."

The lady, unfortunately, already had a lover: Henri Toulouse, away with the colors. The little fatalist came home, learned everything, marched through the streets of Paris, and slapped Guerinet across the face "with the might of a rigor mortis'd sea bass."

They met at Red Phillip's in the second week of January, and the ensuing bout wrecked the club — a chair "completely destroyed, never to be repaired," an heirloom table collapsed, Guerinet scalped bloody (*"My hair! My beautiful hair!"*), Henri gifted "a second set of moustaches" by a cut beneath the nose, and, at the height of it, Guerinet unveiling a spinning strike the proprietor identified with awe — *"The legends were true! Le Ouroboros!"* On the twenty-second round, Guerinet dropped his sword to surrender.

He was a heartbeat too late. Henri's cut was already committed.

> *"Guerinet drops his sword. In the same instant, Henri's blade sweeps his opponent's head clear off his shoulders."*

**Guerinet Durand, 1609–1627** — *lost his head over a woman.* Henri, surveying the remains of his rival mid-surrender, allowed that he could "always deliver his hand to the lady, since it's what she desired." The proprietor protested that Guerinet had been a member in good standing. *"Standing a little shorter now, I suppose,"* mused the house.

> 🎲 **At the table.** This was the winter the game nearly died. Two veteran players had been characterless for months, one openly refusing to reroll until the other did; the GM issued a formal ultimatum — *"the game will end if Jean doesn't make a character by March"* — while a veteran player diagnosed the deeper trouble: the survivors had grown too big to fight each other ("dueling would result in an instagib") and too few to matter. Then the recruitment drive landed — *"YEAH!!! SHOUT OUT FOR A FLOOD OF PEEPZ!!!!!"* — and three new players walked into the meat grinder with their eyes shining: the man history would file under **Joseph Bousain**, then **CitrusNotes**, then **Slade Imbee**. Their first characters' life expectancies are recorded below. Meanwhile the sea-bass duel settled a table record: the player behind Guerinet (and Baschet, and Pierre de Tête) now held the campaign lead in death-by-duel, an honor she greeted with professional composure: *"Well. It didn't work. Time to roll a new character."*

The new blood learned fast, or didn't. Joseph Bousain's first creation, **Robert d'Engaux**, rejected by the Dragoons, rolled into Princess Louisa's Light Dragoons and — via a four-hundred-crown loan and nearly all his savings — bought a senior majority that made him, with the colonel conveniently absent, commander of the whole regiment in his first month. (*"Of course, I will let any talented young men join the dragoons for a small administrative fee."*) His courtship debut set a mark that still stands: needing a six at the formidable Louisiette Flombaux, he rolled a one and improvised, *"Ermmm... hello beautiful Louisiette. I bought you some flowers, but I'm afraid I lost them. Would you like to go to the bawdyhouse with me?"* The Narrator's ruling: *"She will later claim it was a simple clearing of her throat, but you know the truth. You know it was a scoff."*

And in February the war finally said the quiet part aloud. England had seized French naval vessels; the King demanded an answer; the Cardinal forbade open war; and the Minister of War "settled on a softer target" — an unofficial amphibious raid on the island of Jersey and the fishing town of Saint-Hélier. It was a famous victory, meaning: the town was razed, the Cardinal's Guard vanished on a secret mission from which no officer returned, Curtis Sinnoch's elegant three-battalion plan died in a fistfight with boatmen over breakfast, Jules Lavelle's battalion massacred the fled townsfolk in the woods and came away thirteen hundred crowns the richer, and Marius Thibodeaux's Royal Marines rowed into a trap where a toothless old man set the fish-oil-slicked harbor alight and burned boats and swimmers alike. Marius came home haunted by his men "burning like candles against the cold dark night." His own summary was more compact: *"We had a gasoline fight!"*

Two more stones went up. Jaques Peugeot — a bright new arrival of one month's standing, who had vowed "to shake up the city, or die trying" — leapt clear of the flames with great skill, landed on the very edge of the dock, and went through a rickety *plank* into the burning water. **Jaques Peugeot, 1609–1627** — *couldn't stick the landing.* And Clemens Charron — the ox of the fields, slayer of marquis's sons, drinker of Soubise's tea, survivor of four campaigns, the strongest heart in France — took a hunting arrow through that heart while serving as a frontier captain, moments after observing: *"Death... is never far away. It stalks us like a great cat or woodland bear!"* His last words: *"Don't... tell anybody..."* **Clemens Charron, 1611–1627** — *a heart too large for his own good.*

The honors of Jersey went where honors go. Hercule Bonaparte's flawless double envelopment — enjoyed, the record notes, "from far behind the lines... and all of the glory" — confirmed his brigadier-generalcy; his post-battle memorandum analyzing "how a botch of Inconclusive and Pyrrhic Victories can tally up to a Victorious result overall" was entitled, simply, *Synergy*. Curtis Sinnoch's earnings were briefly a barony, until the Narrator's finest retcon in four years of retcons — *"There has been an error in paperwork"* — converted it to the thing he'd wanted since 1625: **Colonel of the Royal Foot Guards**. And Joseph Bousain's Robert, demoted the moment the actual colonel returned, coined the motto of every acting commander in history: *"Absolutely typical of these armchair generals to swoop in and take command after my brave leadership has led to such a fantastic draw."*

## II. The kingmaker at dinner (February–April)

Between campaigns, Paris played its other game, and the spring of 1627 was its masterclass.

A new captain named Pépin Bonheur announced his intent to woo "the Beautiful Mme. Demachy." Word reached Brigadier-General Sir Hercule Bonaparte, whose mistress Alida Demachy was, and whose reply — delivered with "his usual steel-eyed impassiveness" — was a small landmark in the literature of the veiled threat: *obviously* the good captain meant Alida's available and influential sister, since the alternative would be "a Captain offering insult to a Noble General Officer of wealth and means... the path of effective suicide." Pépin pulled at his collar and agreed enthusiastically that he had meant the sister all along.

And Hercule — this is the measure of the man in his final spring — then *helped him*. At dinner with the Demachy sisters he spent twelve crowns and his own considerable presence vouching for the shabby musketeer: *"Armadine, you could do much worse than this lad."* The lady's eyes went wide at her suitor's grand connections; the roll came up exactly what was needed; and Pépin was invited to dinner wearing "a goofy grin because... he had prepared no further words."

The fairy tale lasted five weeks. In April, at Red Phillip's, while Pépin fetched frosty mugs from the bar, the dragoon Robert d'Engaux went down on one knee and slipped a gaudy ring onto tipsy Armadine's finger. *"Bro rules,"* protested Pépin — *"I woo'd her first"* — while the bartender rolled his eyes and, in the chronicle's most efficient stage direction, "begins preparing bandages." The duel that followed was the most patient of the whole war: parry after parry after parry ("we have never had this many parries before," marveled the Narrator), a knee to Robert's jaw that broke teeth, Robert's refusal to yield — *"Never!"* — and at last, on the twenty-first round, his rapier through Pépin's shoulder, blood across the wall, the barkeep standing helpless with his rag. Pépin, at the end of his endurance, surrendered, vomited, and passed out into the nearest chair; Robert courted the lady on the spot, jewelry glittering over fresh bandages, and the Narrator pronounced the union in the season's definitive sentence:

> *"You have won her with blood, and such things are thicker than Hercule's approval."*

> 🎲 **At the table.** Hercule's player was, by his own cheerful confession, running out of cat's paws — *"With Clemens dead, I will need a new cat's paw. Perhaps in the form of a Brevet BG of modest social standing who happens to be Pépin's commanding officer"* — and the whole Demachy-sisters affair unfolded atop a lattice of favours, bribes ("Wow, thanks to Blastagarded's generosity!"), and promised double-dates to Bothwell's. Hercule's off-hand pledge to host "whoever has Armadine's hand on the third week of June 1627" was instantly recognized by its author as hubris: *"Making promises for anything in this game is daring."* It was more daring than he knew: by the third week of June 1627 Hercule Bonaparte had no promises left to keep.

## III. The fires of Chevreuse (May)

The blow fell in the spring. The Duke of Soubise had escaped his captivity — the Cardinal's fury may be imagined — and the English provocateur Walter Montagu had raised him money and men. The new rebel force gathered two days from Paris on the lands of the Duke of Chevreuse, beneath the Château de la Madeleine. The King's army was sent to burn it out.

Burn is the operative word. Hercule Bonaparte, invoking the old logic of the *chevauchée*, had his Dragoons issued incendiaries — *"There is only one thing to do with English who invade our lands and those who tolerate their presence: burn the cancer out"* — and fired the western town. Curtis's Foot Guards took the eastern half and a monstrous twenty-eight hundred crowns in loot; Jules Lavelle fought through the side streets to a bloody lieutenant-colonelcy; Robert d'Engaux detached his squadron, robbed the town's richest merchant under cover of the chaos, acquired a portrait of the King (*"Must've been a Loyalist, sir!"* offered a private, who was executed shortly after "for ermmm.... cowardice"), and rode home a brevet lieutenant colonel. The castle surrendered; the Duke of Chevreuse blamed everything on his wife; Soubise slipped away again.

But the army left three of its own in the ashes, and the chronicle asks the reader to stand for them.

**Pépin Bonheur** — standing *prudently back* from a booby-trapped munitions cache, shielding his eyes from raining debris — never saw "the smoldering, twirling two-by-four, somehow intact after the explosion... It whizzes past, but on the backturn makes solid contact with his skull." The plank's fifth and most baroque kill. *1609–1627: if only his standing had been lower.*

**Henri Toulouse** — who had told Paris on his first day that he foresaw a short life of trouble; who had beheaded a man over Armadine and buried two of his own faces' worth of scars — survived the battle itself. Afterward, he pulled a sobbing girl from beneath her dead family and sheltered her in his own tent; and while he sat engrossed in after-action paperwork, she took his shaving razor and gave him, in the Narrator's words, "the last shave he will ever receive." His player's response, from the far side of the table, deserves the stone it didn't fit on: *"Finally, the warm touch of a belle chère..."* The stone says: **Henri Toulouse, 1609–1627** — *'twas beauty killed the beast.*

And **Hercule Bonaparte**. Minutes earlier — the timing is the point — the King had reviewed the brigadier's petition for a title commensurate with "his incredible upward social mobility" and decided *"this simply will not do"*: too far, too fast, for a merchant's second son. Then, watching his fires consume the town "with ice in his veins," Hercule saw a single hooded rider tear out of the flames. He raised his sword. The rider — the Duke of Soubise himself, making his escape through the inferno Hercule had lit for him — drew a pistol and fired one ball into his chest.

> *"Hercule falls from his horse without a sound, and the Duke flees into the night. The creeping flames overtake the noble Knight, and nothing remains but a dream of ambition."*

**Hercule Bonaparte, 1609–1627** — *his promising career went up in smoke.* He had come to Paris with thirty points of endurance and a merchant's ledger where his heart should have been; he left it a Knight of France, Colonel of the Royal Foot Guards, Brigadier-General of the Dragoon Brigade, blackmailer, kingmaker, keeper of a list of every name in this book — and the only great duelist's mind of his generation never once permitted, by rank or frailty or fate, to fight a duel. His letter to Alida Demachy, written before the campaign and delivered after, left her his entire estate of 2,139 crowns and two instructions: avenge his death "if it needs avenging," and see that none forget the Bonaparte legacy. The Narrator gave the widow-in-spirit the scene the letter deserved:

> *"She betrays no emotion, her hands trembling only slightly. She folds it up crisply and informs her valet to clear her social calendar. She has work to do."*

Curtis Sinnoch threw open Bothwell's to every survivor of "that damned skirmish" to drink his old rival's memory. Jules Lavelle refused the premise outright: *"It cannot be! That man was never less than five steps ahead... When the time is right, he'll return to save us all! Watch and wait!"*

> 🎲 **At the table.** *"What a run,"* wrote Hercule's player, tallying September 1624 to May 1627. *"Fire is a fickle mistress. Hopefully next character I'm not hamstrung with 30 Endurance."* The verdict was unanimous across the table — "you just lost the best character in the game"; *"Seriously, Hercule was the BEST character so far. He did the most intense social climbing"* — and the mourning practical: *"I shall defer rolling [the new character] for a while and use the grief of the loss as an excuse to drink."* The same resolution killed three player characters and dropped the roster to seven; the Narrator instituted an @inactive purge and recruited fresh players within the fortnight. Among the eulogies, someone asked what the campaign had taught. Answer: *"It teaches valuable lessons, such as '3% happens.'"*

## IV. The holy fool and the chicken fanciers (June–July)

What Paris got in Hercule's place was his exact opposite, and one of the most beloved figures of the late war: **Sébastien Lefebvre**, a wide-eyed country boy with a terrible sheet and a soul at perfect peace. He mistook the Gascon Regiment's recruitment for a skills competition and failed it twice — prompting the colonel to double-check "whether the Gascon Regiment had any rejection criteria" (the adjutant's finding: "On paper, Sébastien appears to be the perfect candidate; he is not yet deceased"). He lived contentedly in a communal stable he'd mistaken for lodgings, marveling at the free food in the trough. He fell in with a brigand gang, sincerely believing them to be the night watch, and stood beaming among them while they mugged his future friends (*"We've got a real killer here. Eyes of steel,"* a brigand warned the victim, to general hilarity). Asked by the frontier priest, Father Bergère, how he bore the hardship, the killing, the nearness of death, Sébastien explained that farm work was harder, that a man is "just meat and bone, like everything else," and that dying is God's plan either way — *"Win-win as far as I see it. Are* you *okay, Father?"* The priest poured himself another glass of sacramental wine, unsure whether he had met a simpleton or a faith stronger than his own.

The rest of the new generation scraped for places in a Paris emptied of regiments: Primo Tremblay, first son of a gentleman who "died a red-faced alcoholic," charming his way into the 69th Arquebusiers with a flowery letter, sworn to earn dowries for two unwed sisters; Jerome Greybeck, an impoverished gambler-gentleman with a fine military mind and catastrophic luck, taking the Gascon Regiment's two crowns a month; the earl's son Slade Imbee, too broke for a commission, admitted "due to your excellent standing in society"; and the mistresses themselves thinning — Octavie, Raymonde, and the young heiress Alida Rodier all departing for German courts, their suitors dead or at war.

Into the vacuum came the strangest social phenomenon of the age: with most eligible men on campaign, the young women of Paris took up **competitive chicken breeding**. Alida Demachy, widowed of Hercule, threw herself into the fancy, presenting a prize tri-colored cock — the Right Reverend Raphael Legoinegasque, "with spurs so long he requires a special brace to stand." And Primo Tremblay, panic-buying a suspiciously fluffy hen from knife-twirling non-farmers at market, attended the Chicken Fanciers' meeting and won Armadine Demachy from the absent Robert — being, in the Narrator's deathless formula, *"handsome, chicken-wise, and, above all, present."* Word reached Robert on campaign; the punishments he thereafter administered to innocent privates "involved rather a lot of sword thrusts and parries," dueling being illegal at the front.

## V. Île de Ré (July–August)

Then the English came in person. Thousands of men under **George Villiers, Duke of Buckingham** — the most hated Englishman in France — landed on the Île de Ré to lift the pressure on La Rochelle; only the star fort of Saint-Martin held out. The entire Royal Army embarked to throw him back into the sea.

The battle of the landing produced the chronicle's great tapestry of war: the general's textbook orders foundering in the salt marsh; English dragoons tangled in unseen ropes at the water's edge; disguised "civilians" with pistols nearly gutting the Guards Brigade before a suspicious adjutant's eye saved it; and, bearing down on Curtis Sinnoch's landing site, a **flaming English galleon**, cannons roaring, crazed sailors firing from the burning rigging — until a single disciplined volley of the Royal Foot Guards killed the pilot at the wheel, and the fireship slewed sideways and capsized in the shallows. Jules Lavelle led his battalion wading into the surf to finish the survivors, with the observation that "My family used to summer on the Île de Ré. This is no different from spearfishing my dinner!" Marius Thibodeaux, ordered to scatter his brigade for guerrilla glory, flatly refused — *"We will not split into guerrilla forces so a few commanders can gain glory at the expense of my men's lives"* — and was vindicated by the dice on the spot. Jean Renault, a new major of the Crown Prince Cuirassiers, was challenged to single combat by a crazed enemy captain "in his ugly Germanic tongue," declined with panache (*"A joust? Surely you jest!"*), annihilated the man's troop between two ordered lines, and lifted from the corpse a diamond-studded chivalric cross engraved on the reverse: *Lancelot du Lac*.

The English were driven into the surf. And for the first time in the war's history, the campaign resolved with **zero player deaths** — a fact so unprecedented that a player was seen checking the cemetery channel in disbelief. The honors: a knighthood for Marius (*"Rise, Sir Marius"*), a barony for Curtis, mentions and plunder all around — and then, in the season of favors that followed, the most consequential elevation of the whole chronicle. Curtis Sinnoch stacked his mistress Félice's connections atop his own name, rolled a natural six, and became **Minister of Justice** — whereupon the record notes, in a sentence that should be read twice:

> *"Cardinal Richelieu notes the rising Minister Sinnoch's career with great interest. Words are whispered. Deals are arranged. Curtis Sinnoch has been made a Marquis."*

The last founder was now a Marquis, a Minister, a Colonel of the Royal Foot Guards, and a made man of His Red Eminence.

## VI. La Rochelle, the scarecrows, and the grouse (September–December)

In the autumn the war turned to its final shape: **La Rochelle**, pressured by Buckingham's fleet, opened its gates to the English, and Cardinal Richelieu came in person to direct the siege — engines rising, a blockade closing, "the city's fall only a matter of time." The detached regiments scoured eighty square miles of Aquitaine for the last Huguenot field forces, and Sir Marius Thibodeaux's Royal Marines arrived at the harbor just in time to rebuff a desperate sally and capture, mid-escape, *the Duke of Buckingham himself* — from whose person Marius looted a ducal ring, and from whose captivity France extracted rather more.

The scouring of the farmland produced two immortal absurdities. At the **Battle of the Scarecrows**, the Gascon 3rd Battalion charged a line of enemy infantry that proved to be tactically placed scarecrows, spent its fury bayoneting the silent guardians, and was then shattered at thirty yards by the actual Huguenots concealed beyond — Private Jerome Greybeck surviving a duel with "a particularly menacing scarecrow" to be promoted subaltern over the wreckage. In the same action, the disgraced-in-advance Primo Tremblay hid so effectively that the cleanup crews found him afterward *inside one of the scarecrows*, shivering; the sentence of the court of honor — *"Primo Tremblay has soiled both his trousers and his honor"* — cost him four status points a month until redeemed. And on the aimless perimeter, Sébastien Lefebvre, deciding mid-battle to bag a grouse for dinner, learned what the local grouse had learned about gunfire and meal prospects: they ran *toward* the shot in great numbers. As he admired his kill, one more bird emerged from the underbrush, "softly cooing the last sound he will ever hear," and the holy fool of the Frontier Regiment was torn to shreds by grouse. His final recorded thought: *"petite futée..."* **Sébastien Lefebvre, 1611–1627** — *he was a man of strange fixation; now pioneering aviation.*

Robert d'Engaux fell in the same season, in character to the last: arriving too late to save the Gascons, unable to resist pursuing the fleeing enemy, thrown thirty feet when his horse found a caltrop, landing miraculously unhurt — and surrounded. The enemy moved to take the lieutenant-colonel prisoner. He declined:

> *"You fools! You think you can take me alive! You will never take me al—"*

**Robert d'Engaux, 1609–1627** — *his career took him to new heights.* His player was already rolling the bastard washerwoman's son **Louis Laveuse** by morning, having accidentally also generated a gorgeous SL-15 Norman heiress ("You don't make a new mistress every time in this game, sorry for not explaining" — *"Marie d'Rouen quickly tires of the attractions of Paris and returns to her estate"*).

The honors of the fall ran uphill as ever: **Baron Jules Lavelle** — a title roll of six, a whoop of *"Save a seat for me, Bothwell's!"* — who within the month was grandly lending a hundred crowns to young Jerome with no terms whatsoever (*"What need have gentlemen for terms?"*), then feeling "a strange sense of foreboding" and ignoring it.

And in the year's last weeks, Paris acquired its most peculiar resident. The late Hercule's manor was sold to one **Jean Shawinigan**, a gentleman of vague antecedents who dictated private log entries into a small silver disc, weighed aloud the merits of "life stuck as a wealthy gentleman in the 1600s" against "a temporal prison in the 2800s," and at Red Phillip's noted that "the passive scanners on his neural-link detect that some of the floorboards are composed of more human blood than wood." Paris found him charming. Adélaïde Blaise found him irresistible — with a caveat. Louis Laveuse had won her hand with gallantry and extravagance on a Tuesday; on the Wednesday, Jean Shawinigan privately funded an entire public festival in the park as their first date, and the Narrator recorded the cruelest line in the courting registers:

> *"The lady's heart turns on a crown. 'Louis?' she's heard to say to one of her curious friends. 'Who is Louis? My heart belongs to Jean.'*"

Her sixth and seventh lovers, inside twenty-four hours.

> 🎲 **At the table.** The autumn resolution had been delayed a week because the Narrator "need[ed] to prep for a wedding this week so want to focus on writing a classy best man speech" — to which the indebted CitrusNotes, awaiting his fate at the front, replied: *"I need to know if I'm dead, rich or still poor and delinquent on my loans. Really hoping for the dead or rich options."* (The insolvency rules had been settled months earlier, when another debtor asked: you go to the frontier in Disgrace until the books balance — or, as a fellow player put it, "If you die, you're golden. Debt-free.") Sébastien's death-by-grouse drew the largest reaction wall of the game — doves, stars, laughter, screaming — and his player, mourning his second beloved character in a season, unveiled Jean Shawinigan the *time traveler* within the week, on the impeccable reasoning: *"If I'm going to be stuck in the 1600s, I certainly am not going to do it as a pauper."* The fiction never blinked; the anachronism became a beloved running gag; and the man from the 2800s took his place in the siege lines of the 1620s.

The year closed with the army wintering before La Rochelle, Buckingham in a French cell, a Marquis in the Ministry of Justice, and the north coast quiet. It would not stay quiet: across the Channel, someone was minting new baronets.
