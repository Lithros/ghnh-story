# Chapter I — 1624: The Year of the Croquants

> *"Yet for each young man lost too soon, another two will take his place, lured to Paris by the eternal promises of war and the finest nightlife in the world."*

## I. The arrivals (March)

They announced themselves, as new men in Paris will, too loudly.

The first crop came down the rue in the same week of March 1624 and introduced themselves — this is a matter of documentary record — *in verse*. Phillipe du Kaneda, a bastard with a lecherous papa and unearned confidence, rapped about both. Curtis Sinnoch, son of the Viscount Sinnoch and the only man in the company whose tailor was paid on time, delivered a couplet built around *"Viscount's Son, My Will Be Done."* Pierre de Tête, a well-to-do gentleman's heir with the best sword-arm of his generation and the modesty to match, threatened the assembly in Franglais: *"Check your thread count or you'll en-counter my foil."* Guillaume d'Avergny arrived in blank verse, named for a conqueror, and concluded, surveying the city of his dreams: *"The stench is dreadful."* And a placid Dutch banker introduced himself in haiku:

> *Hi, I'm Pain Loafmann / I'm Dutch so forgive my speech / Blustering Frenchmen*

> 🎲 **At the table.** The founding cohort was rolled up in a 48-hour flurry of 3d6s in August 2020. The very first stat rolls of the campaign were so bad the Narrator's inaugural ruling on his own game was: *"Damn that is some bad luck."* Two christenings from that first night entered legend instantly — the GM screaming **"KANEDAAAAAAA"** at Phillipe's surname, and his one-word review of the bread-pun Dutchman: **"Kojima'd."** Three players rolled bastards in the first week, and a formal **Bastard Fraternity** was proposed, "dedicated to those with lecherous fathers." One member insisted his father was no lecher but had been "seduced by my scheming harpy of a mother"; another revealed that he and his mother were paid a monthly stipend to *be somewhere else*. The scandalized reply — *"You get paid?!"* — tells you most of what you need to know about the year to come.

Alongside these came the rest of the founding ten: Jean-Luc Aveline and Leon Mandelbrot, two common-born second sons of rival merchant houses who took one look at each other and swore eternal friendship-until-rivalry; Godefroy de Beaufosse, a very wealthy gentleman's bastard built of glass and bravado; Etienne de Mercoeur, second son of the Baron de Mercoeur, who stepped off his coach with seven hundred and fifty crowns and the highest station of any of them; and Alphonse Devereux, a gentleman of Reims with the arms of a blacksmith and the lungs of an invalid.

Paris received them with total indifference, which they set about correcting.

Pierre de Tête, on the advice of Phillipe du Kaneda — *"which would you prefer: to be in debt to a noble who needs not your services; or, to have many friends?"* — spent money he did not have hosting the entire cohort at the Frog & Peach, and was rewarded within the month by the Cardinal's own newspaper holding him up as a model of Christian community-building. It was the first entry in what the *Gazette de France* would make of these men: a five-year serial comedy in which they appeared, month after month, under headlines that almost never got anything quite right.

![Gazette de France, March 1624](images/gazette/gazette-de-france_01_1624_March.png)

The recruiting sergeants were less impressed than the Gazette. On the game's first recruitment night the regiments of France turned away nearly everyone: Guillaume d'Avergny was rejected by *seven* regiments in succession — the Royal Marines, the Picardy Musketeers, both Fusilier regiments, the 27th, the 4th, the 69th — a record of futility that would stand until another desperate man equalled it that autumn. When Jean-Luc Aveline was refused later that same night by the 69th and even the lowly Gascons, Guillaume greeted him with the fellowship of the freshly humiliated: *"Welcome to the ranks of the rankless."* Only Etienne de Mercoeur sailed through, straight into the Cardinal's Guard, buying a captaincy on arrival with the shrug of a man for whom money was a rumor about other people: *"Second sons do not have the luxury of a father's aid beyond much more than word and a little investment."*

And already, in that first month, the two institutions that would govern every life in this chronicle made themselves known: the ladies, and the duels they required.

## II. Doorsteps and blood (March–April)

The trouble with courting in Paris was scheduling. Two suitors, one lady, one afternoon: the mathematics resolved themselves with steel.

The first duel of the campaign was fought over Alida Demachy — "the more respectable of the Demachy twins" — when Godefroy de Beaufosse arrived at her door, boots shined and bouquet in hand, to find Jean-Luc Aveline already occupying the step in an identical condition. Cutlass against sabre. On the second exchange Godefroy's cutlass shattered against a block — *"How fragile these implements are!"* — and shortly thereafter Jean-Luc opened him up so thoroughly that Mlle. Demachy nearly fainted at what the *Gazette* later called "the crescent of blood which now decorates her home's facade." Godefroy yielded with the grace of a natural philosopher: *"It seems a man's core is a messy place, full of fluids and stench!"*

*"I have found,"* replied Jean-Luc, *"that crossing blades with a man is sometimes the best way to get to his core."* He then knocked on the lady's door, bloody sabre still in hand, and was accepted. The Narrator's entry in the record is a small masterpiece of the period: *"Off they go, arm in arm, Jean-Luc's bloody saber the perfect memento of their first rendez-vous."*

The month's other doorstep produced the campaign's first *true match*: Leon Mandelbrot and Etienne de Mercoeur, rapier on rapier, nineteen turns, both men soaked red before Etienne yielded — and then, showing his breeding, shook his conqueror's hand and invited him to Hunter's. Whereupon the famously beautiful Adélaïde Blaise rejected the victor anyway. The Narrator ruled her reasoning thus: *"Utterly disappointed at your failure to kill Etienne, she soundly rejects you."*

*"Madame,"* protested Leon, bleeding on her steps, *"I am a gentleman, not a murderer!"* — a distinction Mlle. Blaise never did learn to appreciate. She refused Curtis Sinnoch the same month (the dice were merciless: *"looks like Class can't buy class"*), then accepted him in April, beginning her long career as the most consequential heartbreaker in Paris. The poets fared worst of all. Guillaume d'Avergny recited flower-petal verses to the wealthy Héloïse Giteau and was informed she "would be moved by the beauty of the words, were they not uttered by a pauper." Phillipe du Kaneda, addressing the same lady as "Lady Gâteau," compared her to a moist cake — *"I would savor every morsel of you"* — and was, incredibly, also rejected.

April brought commissions and, with them, tribal hatred. Pierre de Tête borrowed a hundred crowns from Curtis Sinnoch — the loan was negotiated at Hunter's over Portuguese port, while the whole club fell silent at an unwary mention of Cardinal Richelieu — and splashed six hundred and ten crowns on a junior *majority* in Grand Duke Max's Dragoons, plus the three horses the dignity required. Jean-Luc Aveline became a private in the Archduke Leopold Cuirassiers, was issued a brush and a pitchfork, and was ordered to report two weeks a month for stable duty. And since the Dragoons and the Cuirassiers were Enemy Regiments, Pierre and Jean-Luc were now obliged to duel more or less on sight.

> *"As if having competing family businesses wasn't enough!"* — Pierre de Tête, on discovering his best customer was now his sworn enemy

They met within the month, sabre against sabre, and it was an education. Jean-Luc mistimed a parry on the fourth turn and took a wound that would have killed most of the men in this book — fifty-two points of it — before clawing back; Pierre finished the affair *standing on a table* ("Proper form for cavalry, after all," observed the Narrator) and invited him to yield: *"You've already redecorated the place!"* The loser consoled himself with philosophy: his mistress would rather grieve a scar than a corpse.

> 🎲 **At the table.** By late August 2020 — game-time April 1624 — the players had located every edge of the rules and begun filing at them. Could a dueling blade be poisoned? *"The rules do not account for such an underhanded, scoundrelly tactic."* Could you fight the footpads outside the bawdyhouse? No: *"they bring enough to guarantee you will lose,"* though they never kill — *"they know that's bad for business."* And the exchange that should be carved over the door of the whole campaign: **"Does a mare count as a female companion?"** — *"only on campaign."*

## III. The rebellion (May–August)

It was the salt tax, in the end, that gave the year its name. The Croquants — the peasant leagues of the southwest, whom the *Gazette* called "the filthy Jacques" — rose under two leaders, Donat and Barran, and the frontier began to burn.

The first to march were volunteers. Guillaume d'Avergny, broke, humiliated in love ("more than I could bear — not just emotionally, but financially"), joined the frontier regiments as a common ranker; Pierre de Tête brought his 3rd Squadron of dragoons. In May the rebels assaulted the frontier works, the colonel commanding rolled up a plan the record calls idiocy, and the position fell apart. Pierre — receiving his orders from "a muddy, shoeless child" and recognizing the defensive plan "for the sheer idiocy it is" — led his squadron in a fighting withdrawal, the only command on that field to ride out intact.

Guillaume d'Avergny was standing in the front line when the battlement in front of him blew apart.

> *"Is this the pale hand of Death before me?"* he asked, and it was. **Guillaume d'Avergny, 1607–1624** — the chronicle's first grave. His sword was returned from the front and set aside in the post office for his family to claim. It sat in the corner for years, rusting, until it was thrown away during a renovation.

> 🎲 **At the table.** Guillaume was the Narrator's *own* player character — the GM ran his creations through the same grinder as everyone else, and the grinder did not care. His live commentary as the battle result came in: *"Pierre has a chance of salvaging it a bit, I'm screwed... I'll die if I roll above a 5."* He rolled a 10. The table's eulogy was tender: *"Eh, f— 'em,"* said one player, *"I didn't like the guy anyways."* — *"He was kind of a stick-in-the-mud,"* agreed the bereaved. Within a game-month the Narrator's replacement character strode into Paris: **Jules Lavelle, son of the Marquis Lavelle**, introduced with the immortal *"You had better say so, or my father the Marquis will have words with your regimental commander."* One player observed that the past campaign had been "a big advertisement for going on campaigns." — *"That's why,"* said the GM, *"it's not a choice in the summer."*

It was not a choice in the summer. The frontier defeat made Donat and Barran famous, the rebellion threatened to spread, and the King charged the Minister of War to raise a great army — six brigades in three armies, which is to say very nearly every man in this chronicle. Paris emptied. Letters went back and forth all season: Pierre wrote his mother about wet socks (*"P.S. How's the family business doing?"*); Leon Mandelbrot exchanged daily letters with his new-won Laurence Caillat and spent a week in quiet panic when one went astray; Jean-Luc Aveline pointedly did *not* write to Alida Demachy, brooding instead on the two hundred and forty crowns she had already cost him, his tears — the record is specific — falling onto his boots and "washing a small area clean of horse excrement."

The army found the rebels in August. What followed was the bloodiest single night of the entire war, and it began, as such nights do, at headquarters. General Gilles Barbier — *"famed for his politics and never his prowess"* — ordered a frontal assault across open ground.

The Archduke Leopold Cuirassiers charged and were massacred. Jean-Luc Aveline, who had said the rosary at dawn and made his peace with God, chose that morning to be reckless beyond all sense — he had told the world *"For France!"* and amended it, mid-charge, to *"For Glory!"* — and did not come back. The Princess Louisa's Light Dragoons were held back by their colonel's cunning wait-and-see, which proved to be exactly wrong; Godefroy de Beaufosse was pulled down from his mount and torn to shreds by bloodthirsty peasants. The Cardinal's Guard were isolated and crushed, and Etienne de Mercoeur — the richest, best-born, most golden of the founding ten — died under "dozens of stabs from rusty farm implements," his fine armor counting for nothing at all.

And in the defensive lines, where the 53rd Fusiliers stood, the rebels heaved a huge wooden plank up and over the wall. It crushed the skull of a young private on the way down; the regiment was overrun. Leon Mandelbrot survived the collapse and was promoted captain over the bodies of four of his predecessors. Private Phillipe du Kaneda was not so lucky: a second plank found him, *"just as the old gypsy woman predicted."*

Four graves in one night: **Jean-Luc Aveline** (*buried with his spade*), **Godefroy de Beaufosse** (*never a poltroon*), **Etienne de Mercoeur** (*remembered by the Cardinal*), **Phillipe du Kaneda** (*crushed by a plank; I bet that stank*).

The one bright thread that night belonged to Pierre de Tête, who took his squadron forward with deliberate, calculated recklessness, survived it, and rolled the kind of glory men lie about: Mentioned in Despatches, the news of his courage sent ahead to Paris, all the city abuzz — and fifty crowns lifted from a dead peasant leader's purse. *"I raise his dead hand,"* he reported, *"and also use it to pat myself on the back."* The sacrifice of the first division opened the rebel flank; the pincer closed; the rebel army was, in the chronicle's words, wiped from the surface of the earth.

The *Gazette*'s September issue declared GLORIOUS VICTORY, credited "M. de Tête, whose gallant and valorous contributions proved decisive," and noted "miraculously low casualties," the mass funerals for which were held on fourteen separate days of the month.

![Gazette de France, September 1624](images/gazette/gazette-de-france_04_1624_Septembre.png)

> 🎲 **At the table.** The summer of 1624 established the game's two great superstitions. The first was the **plank**: two player characters brained by lumber in a single campaign produced a custom `:plank:` emoji that reacted to every death, danger, and act of carpentry for the next two years, and a standing benediction — *"To those going to war, beware of wooden planks!"* The second was the theology of **Dice Golem**. *"Being nice to Dice Golem hasn't really worked,"* one mourner reasoned. *"Maybe it just wants to be slapped around a bit. Some bots are into that."* The mechanics beneath the carnage: on a bad battle result a private died on a quarter to more than half of all possible rolls, and the *Reckless Bravery* option — trading a worse death number for better odds of glory — killed Jean-Luc exactly as efficiently as it decorated Pierre. Offered the safer alternative, *Poltroonery*, one player replied: **"You spelled pragmatism wrong."**

## IV. The regeneration (September)

Paris did not stay empty. Within weeks the coaches delivered the second generation, and the survivors learned their names.

In place of Jean-Luc came **Guy de Cavoye**, bastard son of an earl, compulsive gambler, high of blood and empty of purse (*"I actually have less money than before,"* his maker marveled). In place of Godefroy came **Clemens Charron**, and all Paris stared: a peasant's first son with the strongest back and biggest heart in France, walking into society with a windfall of exactly one crown. In place of Etienne came **Henri Toulouse**, a small merchant's son of small means and smaller stature, who surveyed his own prospects with the fatalism of a man reading his own epitaph in advance: *"I see a short life of trouble in my future."* And in place of Phillipe, his maker produced a frail, brilliant creature the record calls a "fragile military genius" — a merchant's son with the constitution of wet paper and the finest military mind of his generation, whose name Paris would come to know, and fear a little, as **Hercule Bonaparte**.

> 🎲 **At the table.** The mourning rituals of the server were now codified. A player whose character died renamed himself for the interval of grief — the dice bot faithfully recorded rolls by *"Phillipe [regenerating],"* *"Ghost of Godefroy de Beaufosse,"* and *"Etienne de Phantome"* — making Dice Golem an accidental obituary column. Posting his new sheet, Phillipe's player caught a leftover field from the dead man's regiment and could not finish the sentence: *"Correction: Not in the 53rd. Forgot to clear if from... from..."* Three ghost emoji answered him. Meanwhile harry, the night after his gentleman died, opened his reroll with triple fives and built the ox-hearted Clemens — prompting the GM to name the new arc on the spot: **"From Rags to Regrets."**

The autumn belonged to Hercule Bonaparte, who demonstrated in his first six weeks that wars are won before they are fought. Item: a noble was seen furiously hurling jewelry into a park (it was Jules Lavelle, freshly rejected; the crystal necklace had failed to sway Mlle. Caillat and had lately, per the Gazette's WANTED column, gone missing from her windowsill). Hercule's servant "found" it, and Hercule composed an exquisitely polite letter offering its return in exchange for entrée to Hunter's — settled, after negotiation, for an evening as Jules's guest and the necklace's destruction. *"Consider the matter dust in the wind."* Item: Alida Demachy, newly bereft of Jean-Luc, suffered the worst day of her life — every small thing going wrong from morning on — until, at her lowest hour, Hercule Bonaparte appeared before her, wealth on full display, offering his hand; the record notes he hoped "she never realizes she is exactly where he planned her to be." She accepted. Item, and least explicable: a private notation in Hercule's journal, uncovered centuries later during the Paris reconstruction, consisting of nothing but a list of names. Every name in this chronicle. Jules Lavelle, reading over the historian's shoulder as it were, shivered "as if someone has just walked over his grave."

And item, in the fullness of time confessed: two anonymous pouches of fifty crowns each, delivered to the home of the penniless Clemens Charron, immediately before Clemens's already-strong public opinions about Jules Lavelle sharpened into a dueling matter.

## V. Lions and fawns (October–December)

While the captured rebel leaders stood trial that September, the year's great argument broke into the open in the streets — Jules the marquis's son on one side, the peasant Clemens Charron on the other, arguing salt.

> *"Take a man's salt and you've taken so much from him — the flavor in his food, the sweat off his back, and one of the few luxuries in his cupboard."* — Clemens Charron
>
> *"Villains are as villeins do, bear in mind."* — Jules Lavelle

In October they hanged Donat and Barran before city hall, the Cardinal presiding from a window with a speech about sacrificing his own soul for France. Leon Mandelbrot denounced the rebels for killing his friends and handed Jules Lavelle a tomato; Jules threw it with gusto, "missing widely" — while Clemens, in the crowd, kept the quarrel warm. It escalated for weeks more, through lion-and-fawn metaphors of increasing menace — *"When you spy a lion a-shiver, show concern for the fawn,"* offered Jules; *"I will be as clear as ice, my dear Lavelle: I do hope to see more lions freeze,"* returned Clemens — with Hercule Bonaparte lounging within earshot, wondering aloud how a Major who tolerated ridicule from peasants proposed to defend France. Whether the fifty-crown pouches were doing the talking is a question the chronicle leaves to the reader. Jules challenged; Clemens accepted — *"Blades first, libations second!"*

The duel was hosted, with superb Lavelle logic, at Jules's own club, as his own dinner party: the challenged man invited as his guest so the damages fell under his dues, the doors opened to the street so the people of Paris should not miss a thrashing, and the wine bottles carried out first, "since wasting wine would be a worse disgrace than refusing to fight." Over his own estate's vintage, Clemens observed that it reminded him of the liquors "we would make at home as children... mucking out barns." A retraction was demanded and refused. They fought before the open doors, and the strongest body in France beat the marquis's son with the same thrusting lunge delivered three times — *"It seems lightning can strike thrice"* — while the Narrator's framing of the matchup passed instantly into legend:

> *"One, the embodiment of the strength, vitality, and courage that personify the French noble image. The other, a Lavelle."*

*"My fencing lessons,"* wheezed Jules, surrendering on the twelfth turn, *"...did not cover this."* The two men were oddly cordial ever after, in the way of a certain kind of Frenchman who respects nothing so much as a public beating.

The autumn had its wars too, though smaller ones. The fall volunteers — Curtis Sinnoch with the Queen's Own Carabiniers, Alphonse Devereux with the Royal Marines — besieged the Spanish forts of the Valtellina, an affair of repelled assaults and grapnels that found no purchase, remembered chiefly for Colonel Rémy Aymard charging the gates screaming *"My life for France!"* (the gates declined), for Guy de Cavoye's rueful observation that his highest roll of the night had been for death, and for Alphonse prying an ancient coat-of-arms off a fortress wall during the withdrawal and selling it in Paris for two hundred crowns. The *Gazette* reported the fort eventually fell "once the defenders ran out of food," which the chronicle records as the most honest sentence it ever printed. Curtis had ridden out wearing a freshly won Regimental Adjutant's sash; Jules Lavelle and Pierre de Tête were made Brigade Majors — *"pretty easy [to climb] when you're a Brigade Major,"* as the record dryly notes — and Pierre, with a beaver-fur muff and a letter inviting a lady to the grape harvest at Montmartre, won the elegant Sidonie Antier. Alphonse Devereux won Raymonde Haillet the same season with an entire shopping expedition of New World imports; her beaverskin hat, per the *Gazette*, set off a fashion epidemic. Jules Lavelle, courting Suzanne LeBeau with the directness of a cavalry manual, was rejected on the grounds that the lady was — the Narrator's ruling survives — *"not much one for cheese."*

![Gazette de France, October 1624](images/gazette/gazette-de-france_05_1624_Octobre.png)

December closed the year at the Frog & Peach, where Pierre de Tête threw a Christmas party "à la Fezziwig," complete with a proposed holiday amnesty on regimental duels, *for the ladies*. The evening produced the year's finest guest: a cloaked stranger nursing a drink beside an oddly fragrant canvas bag, who proved to be Jean-Luc Devereux, Alphonse's father, come up from Reims to see how "my little Alphonse is doing in the big city." He carved a seasoned lamb for the whole club; sang a bawdy carol of his own invention (*"Noël, Noël / The angel Noël / Was the name of my first / And she taught me so well"*); recognized Curtis as the son of his very first investor, the Viscount "Reynard" Sinnoch, "since not a single hen in Paris was safe"; scolded Hercule Bonaparte for taking notes at a party; bet the table maximum "all for my little Alphonse," lost it, and roared: *"I'll tell my wife it was cutpurses that set upon me. Close enough!"* Every reveler found ten crowns and a slice of lamb slipped into his purse on the way out.

Days later he was dead in the Rue Saint-Jacques — "rent in twain by some massive cutting implement," said the physicians. On his doublet: a dusting of breadcrumbs.

It was not the first such killing. The *Gazette* had been tracking the pattern since September under the name **the Stale Killer** — victims taken unawares by a large blade, a bit of stale baguette left at each scene, one survivor reporting only that the perpetrator "seems to have an accent." The Royal Marines took over the investigation. Paris locked its doors. And in the street outside, Pain Loafmann — flush with gambling winnings, cheerfully oblivious to the grief around him, demonstrably foreign of accent, and never without the oversized scabbard containing his family's ancestral *brotzweihander*, a two-handed bread-sword before which "the loaves, no matter how old, stale or crusty, all fall to pieces at the mere touch" — expressed his condolences.

> *"Will no one save our city from this lingering shadow of Pain and Destruction?"* — the Gazette de France, December 1624, in a sentence its editor swore was innocent

![Gazette de France, December 1624](images/gazette/gazette-de-france_07_1624_Decembre.png)

The year ended with the army wintering on the Breton coast, a Huguenot duke named Soubise assembling a navy in secret, and — in a warm corner of the 27th Musketeers' mess that he would never deign to occupy — Hercule Bonaparte failing, at his first asking, to argue his way into the Crown Prince Cuirassiers, the only regiment in France he considered worthy of him. Offered a majority in the 27th instead, he declined it in the tone of a man declining a second helping of something regrettable:

> *"I could also drink swine piss. But I will not."*

He got his Cuirassiers in the new year, at the third attempt, and spent six hundred and forty crowns on the majority and three horses before the ink dried. *"At last,"* he said, *"I can prove my mettle."* The new year would give every man in Paris the chance.
