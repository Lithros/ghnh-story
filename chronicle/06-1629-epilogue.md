# Chapter VI — 1629: The Last Campaign

> *"The Minister of War is especially pleased to learn that everyone has volunteered!"*

## I. Fireworks (January)

The last courtship of the war belonged, fittingly, to the gunpowder man.

Angeline Gensoul was facing her twenty-first birthday without a beau. Her family's strange reputation for mysticism had curdled, in the clubs, into talk of a *curse* — some fate that visited itself upon her suitors — and the wiser gentlemen of Paris had concluded that other ladies existed. Albert Blastagarde was not a wiser gentleman. He appeared at her door brushing snow from his cape and made the one proposal no one had thought to try: that she become his *pretty assistant* in the arts of gunpowder and weaponry — laboratory, battlefield, and "wonderful dinners" inclusive.

On his signal, the sky went up. The Narrator gave the scene the full grandeur of the age: snowflakes spiraling upward on the heat of the fireworks, glittering as they melted and refroze, "and even as the roofs of nearby houses catch fire, the flames of her heart are no less roused." The die came up six. Albert, gesturing vaguely at a burning building, closed the deal with the last courting line of the game:

> *"Don't worry about that. You'll get used to that."*

He had one month with her. In the first week of February his orders came, and Captain Blastagarde kissed his new beau farewell as his carriage was loaded with prototypes — his refugee-inspection company already famous for its walls of cannons, its personal cannon for any soldier who requested one, its experimental five-javelin auto-loading miniature ballista. The final large box required four men to lift. It bore a simple marking:

> **Le Vaporisateur**

## II. The Creuse (February–March)

The Duke of Rohan — brother of Soubise, last great captain of the Protestant cause — was marching his surviving armies south along the royal highway where it runs beside the river Creuse. The Minister of War called for volunteers for one final winter campaign, and got a response unique in the annals: everyone.

> *"As BG General, I'm voluntolding EVERYONE I CAN."* — Curtis Sinnoch
>
> *"Woohoo! I don't have to think about my orders."* — a captain of the 1st Brigade

Brigadier-General Sinnoch's plan was his masterpiece: cavalry harassment on the flanks, supply lines sabotaged, a deceptively inviting ford, and the Royal Foot Guards hidden at the treeline above it. The exhausted Huguenot column walked into it almost blind, and the opening volleys were a massacre — "The river runs red with the blood of their comrades."

Then the battle did what battles in this war always did. A delayed Picardy charge leapt directly into the Foot Guards' officer-hunting volley and took dozens of French balls in the back. Jules Lavelle's furious counter-charge collapsed when his horse tripped over a slain Musketeer, and Rohan's cavalry slaughtered the Guardsmen who rushed to save their colonel. The Frontier Regiment cornered the enemy against the river by the book — and the Protestants fought "like demons" and cut a bloody swathe to freedom. Jean Renault, wisely ignoring the Frontier Regiment's orders, sacrificed his own squadron to peel away Rohan's reinforcements, out-generaling his superiors at the price of nearly everything he commanded.

And Le Vaporisateur fired. Six full cannon barrels on an aiming mechanism, loaded with exploding shot, "requiring four men to operate and one brave soul to be standing next to it when it fired" — the brave soul being its inventor. Two balls burst in their barrels. Three landed amid the densest Protestant ranks, with effects the record declines to soften: "Weapons, limbs, and surprised faces, some still attached to their heads, fly in every direction." The last skipped across the river and broke the legs of the waiting French cavalry's horses "like brittle twigs."

Albert Blastagarde stood in the smoke of his opus, reveling in visions of a future where no man could leave a trench without being *vaporized* — and did not see what the bursting barrels had done to his scaffolding. A single truss plank, torn loose, "slowly, implacably, inevitably descends on its inventor, who is, far from vaporized, flattened by his inability to get out of the way of progress."

The plank — which had crushed Phillipe du Kaneda in the first summer of the war, drowned Leon Mandelbrot at Blavet, dropped Lucien Vaillancourt off a fortress wall, brained Pépin Bonheur on the ricochet, and arrived at Marius Thibodeaux's beach wearing an entire galleon — had come, at the last, for the house of Blastagarde itself, which had started the whole joke. **Albert Blastagarde, 1612–1629** — *a crushing loss to military science.* His player's one-word verdict on the death roll: *"Worth."*

Two more stones closed the cemetery. Captain Armand Dufour — grazed, trampled, and concussed by Le Vaporisateur's shockwave, and taking this as "an irrefutable message from on high" that luck was with him — led his remnant along the river, saw a lone rider approaching, assumed reinforcements, and raised his arm in greeting. "In response, the Duke of Rohan swings his saber, lopping off the offending limb as he rides off down the river." He bled out into the Creuse, his last recorded concern being whether the bloodstains on his uniform could really *all* be his. He never learned what had become of his vanished father — or whether the man was his father at all — nor that, if a certain vanished time traveler's databases can be believed, he was alive, and destined to outlive his son by decades. **Armand Dufour, 1612–1629** — *always ready to lend a hand.*

And Subaltern Slade Imbee — the earl's son who had come to Paris to win back his family's name, who had volunteered for every front that would have him — met a distinguished enemy officer in the fatal mud, rapier against rapier-and-lit-hand-cannon. He twisted impossibly aside from the shot, threw mud in the officer's face, tackled him, and choked him out with his bare hands. "Just as the life goes out of the officer's eyes, an explosive shell from another experimental device impacts directly on Slade's back. He, and his victory, are never known again." **Slade Imbee, 1610–1629** — *undefeated in battle.* The stone, for once, is simply true.

Rohan escaped south. The dispatches, when they came, were the last of the war: Curtis Sinnoch — who had spent the battle on a hidden scaffold directing runners, taking pot shots, and drafting condolence letters over bottles of fine Chablis — was Mentioned, promoted Brevet Lieutenant-General, handed a 2,100-crown bonus "for your initiative and genius in trimming the ranks of the Frontier Regiment in the course of victory," and created an **Earl**. (*"Huzzah! Got it!"*) Jules Lavelle, his string of sixes restored at the hour it mattered, was made **Colonel of the Royal Foot Guards** — the chair Hercule had held, and Curtis after him, now his. Joseph Bousain — who had fought under the false name "Albert Demachy" and spent the battle stealing ammunition from Le Vaporisateur to lob at the Huguenots by hand — was promoted Subaltern of the 13th Fusiliers once the paperwork error was corrected, which in this army constitutes a distinguished career. Abelard Soucy, seventh of his line, survived without a scratch: *"Abelard's time in the army turns out to be as anticlimactic as his time in Paris"* — the gentlest fate ever recorded for a Soucy character, and therefore, by the family's standards, the strangest.

Jean Renault survived too — and to him fell the tontine his dead squadron had put together: seventeen hundred crowns, and the honor of speaking the last line of the war:

> *"Party at the horse guards for those who survived!"*

## III. The silence

There is no March 1629 in the record.

The announcements channel, which had turned every month for twenty-two real months — through weddings, births, dental emergencies, and a global pandemic — posted "February 1629" on the 5th of June, 2022, resolved the Winter Campaign over the days that followed, laid three final stones in the cemetery, and stopped. No proclamation ended the game; it ended the way the best campaigns do, mid-breath, with the survivors still rich in unspent plans: Curtis within reach of a marshal's baton, Jules newly enthroned in the Foot Guards, Joseph Bousain still looking for his father, Angeline Gensoul's curse still unbroken, Rohan still at large, and the Bastille — as one player would point out, years later — only a hundred and sixty years from its appointment with the descendants of every peasant the Frontier Regiment ever trampled.

> 🎲 **At the table.** The last quip in the table-talk channel before the long silence was posted on June 8th, 2022, over a meme about French governance: *"I prefer 'merdeocracy.'"* (In-character play ran two days longer, ending with the Horse Guards toast.) Then two years of quiet — until July 9th, 2024, when a bot joined the server, and the Narrator explained: *"don't get too excited. just a bot to help me extract all the message history. hoping to use AI to process it into a narrative."* (The document in your hands is the distant descendant of that message.) He added that he wouldn't rule out running the game again — with better automation, and a plan to "vastly expand recruitment." The old guard surfaced within the hour, led by the man who had played Kaneda, Hercule, Sébastien, Shawinigan, and Blastagarde: he greeted the news with *"And that, friends, is the story of how Hercule Bonaparte threw a monkey wrench into AI alignment,"* confessed he'd been "nostalgically thinking about this server for the past few months," and then posted a mock copyright reservation over his characters, valid "in any jurisdiction, both known and unknown, within the universe, as well as any jurisdiction which may exist outside the universe," to secure his *character created by* credit against the Hollywood adaptation. The Narrator's last recorded ruling, asked why the game was ending short of the Revolution: *"1625, 1789, what's the difference."*

## IV. The reckoning

Five game-years. Twelve players and their Narrator. **Thirty-one graves.**

Of the ten founders of March 1624, one character — one — survived the entire war: **Curtis Sinnoch**, Earl, Brevet Lieutenant-General, Marquis by the Cardinal's whisper, Minister of Justice, Knight, Colonel-emeritus of the Royal Foot Guards, owner of the finest falcon and worst dagger-arm in Paris, who buried every friend of his youth and never once stopped auditing the wine.

The rest is the cemetery's story, and the cemetery tells it better than anyone — thirty-one stones, each with a joke where the psalm should be, because that was always the house style. They are collected in [Appendix B](appendix-b-necrology.md), and the reader is encouraged to walk the rows.

The game's own epitaph was spoken years before it ended, by the Narrator, explaining the world to a newcomer:

> *"Yet for each young man lost too soon, another two will take his place, lured to Paris by the eternal promises of war and the finest nightlife in the world."*
