# Chapter V — 1628: The Year of the Long Siege

> *"#campaign is just the waiting room for #cemetery."*
> — a mourner, spring 1628

## I. The duel of the ages (January)

It began, as the great ones always did, over Adélaïde Blaise.

Louis Laveuse — the washerwoman's son with an earl's blood and a chip the size of the Louvre — spotted his lost beloved shopping on the arm of Jean Shawinigan, the serene gentleman of no known past, and demanded satisfaction. The town knew before the principals did; a crowd had assembled at Red Phillip's before either man arrived. Jean came first, Mlle. Blaise on his arm ("thoroughly unimpressed with the place"), and drank brandy with perfect calm. Louis arrived insultingly late — *"this err... establishment was not familiar to me. Let's get this over with, so I and the lady can return to somewhere more... upscale"* — through ripples of muttered *coward*.

*"Just because we're about to maul each other,"* said Jean, insisting they finish their drinks first, *"doesn't mean we can't enjoy ourselves first."* Then he turned his back on his challenger and winked at the lady.

The fight was brief and terrible. A standing high kick splintered Louis's cheekbone on the second exchange; Louis answered with a spirited lunge and a knee where knees are not gentlemanly; and on the eleventh round Jean's cut opened him for the worst single stroke the club had ever hosted — "the spray of blood coats everyone within Red Philips, a perfect crimson circle." Louis looked down at himself and delivered the most seventeenth-century last words in this entire history:

> *"Mon dieu! Look what you have done to my coat. Father will be so angry."*

**Louis Laveuse, 1605–1628** — *lived like he had nothing to lose.* The proprietor facepalmed — *"Not another one!"* — and Jean, in what the record calls "neutral compassion," said only: *"I tried to warn you, my friend."* His eulogy, delivered to Adélaïde on the walk home, is the coldest tenderness in the chronicle: the dead man had died for her; Jean would only ever *kill* for her, and return alive — "and one action does have longer term prospects than the other."

> 🎲 **At the table.** The challenge had been issued in the wrong channel entirely — *"I HEREBY CHALLENGE YOU TO A DUEL GOOD SIR!"* / *"Wrong channel"* — and the man behind Jean Shawinigan was giddy for a reason that took the table a moment to register: across four characters and nearly four years, from Phillipe du Kaneda onward, **he had never once fought a duel**. Hercule, the great schemer, had dodged, delegated, and died without ever crossing steel. His heir did it once, and killed. The duel was also a technology demo: Jean's "neural link" ran threat assessment on his challenger (*"Threat assessment: Limited — Temporal Relevance: Negligible"*), calendared the appointment as "Give a beatdown to some rando," and activated implanted sobriety meds before he drew.

The killer spent the rest of the month in character. Finding young Jerome Greybeck at the tables — solvent for one shining evening and still two hundred and twenty crowns short of his debts, packing for the Frontier Regiment where insolvent gentlemen went in Disgrace — a deeply drunk Jean pulled him aside and delivered the greatest monologue in the history of Red Phillip's: a slurred, profane, weirdly prophetic analysis of the age (*"You know where we are? We're in the sixteen-fuckin'-hundreds! That's where we are. That's where we're sstuck"*; *"You're either born at the shit end of the stick or you're born at the marginally less shit end of the stick"*), a warning off the gambling tables delivered via a ballad he attributed vaguely to the future (*"You gotta know when to hold them and know when to fold them"*), and the verdict that a patronless man's only road was "to risk it all on the battlefield and hope you don't get your head blown off." Departing with the visibly impatient Mlle. Blaise, Jean — "whether intentionally or in his drunken stupor" — left his coin purse on the bar. Two hundred and fifty crowns. Jerome's debts, cleared with change to spare.

The battlefield took its payment anyway. In February the English tried their maddest stroke of the war — a swarm of freshly minted baronets landing in Normandy to *kidnap King Louis himself* — and the winter army that rode to stop them amid the hedgerows collected two of the young men of Paris. Jerome Greybeck, leading a doomed charge behind a colonel who had taken a musket ball to the cranium and never recovered ("soaked in his own spittle"), reached the hedgerow itself and died tangled in its vines, eating a last handful of berries that were, of course, poisonous — "but you must have blood in your body for that to matter." **Jerome Greybeck, 1609–1628** — *his debts came due.* And Primo Tremblay, the scarecrow poltroon, rode out to redeem his honor with deliberate recklessness, stood steady at last, unafraid — and was dragged to death behind his own spooked horse through a field of thistles. "What returns to camp... is not recognizable as a soldier or a man, but the grooms know the horse well." **Primo Tremblay, 1609–1628** — *ground into the ground, now into the ground.* His sisters' dowries died with him.

The baronets fled to their boats, "having learned their lesson about French force-of-arms." Jean Renault came out of the hedgerows with a full lieutenant-colonelcy. The rest of France began looking west, to the city Richelieu was slowly strangling.

## II. The slaughter (March–May)

The spring levy of 1628 was fed by a new English scheme: the Baron of Nayland, First Lord of the Admiralty, packing swarms of Cornish fishing boats with hardened sea dogs — "many of whom saw their first service under Sir Francis Drake himself" — to run the La Rochelle blockade at the beaches. Curtis Sinnoch, given overall deployment, produced a genuinely elegant ambush plan and personally drove his sector's enemy into the sea. But down the coast, the Frontier Regiment stood with its eyes fixed inland while "Reavers, echoing the Northmen of old" rose out of the surf behind them and slit the entire rear rank's throats.

When the tide went out, the game had suffered the worst single resolution in its history: **four player characters dead in one evening.**

**Sir Marius Thibodeaux** — brevet brigadier-general, knight, captor of Buckingham, the fastest riser the army ever saw, who had gone to this war with the elite Royal Northern Highlands Brigade because, as he put it, *"I think I've fairly thoroughly killed off anyone who looks to me as a leader at this point"* — whispered *"Angélique....."* over the dice, carved a fighting retreat to the beach, set his back to the sea, and watched the reavers break and run before French valor. "They never see the galleon rise up out of the surf behind them, carried high on a freak wave, slamming down into the gathered Frenchmen below. Onlookers report a loud slapping sound of wood on sand, and then eerie silence." The plank had learned to scale. **Marius Thibodeaux, 1608–1628** — *his ship had just come in.* Kilometers away, a Marquis lowered his spyglass and observed, before the sand had settled: *"There has been... a vacancy that needs to be addressed."* (The Cardinal concurred that a Marquis outranks a Knight; Sinnoch was brevetted into the dead man's stars.)

**Jean Shawinigan** — who had stepped onto the battlefield against every protocol of his… academy, muttering *"Survive first. Fix later."* — led a devastating charge, disarmed an elegant English captain with a foot-stomp and a shoulder-shove, and raised his blade for the coup de grâce. Nearby soldiers reported a bright light, strange words concerning a *temporal paradox*, and then nothing: the man dissolved into particles of dissipating light, "leaving nothing behind but an astonished English captain." Within weeks, no one in Paris would admit he had ever existed; the only trace was a street vagrant's tale of *Great Jean the Timeless*, who "bested forty men before ascending to a higher temporal plane." **Jean Shawinigan, ?–1628** — *out of time.*

**Anton Dechets and Pierre d'Amboise** were killed by the same cannonball. Anton — *"an absolute trash person,"* by his own maker's proud description, a bastard storyteller who had achieved the campaign's unique distinction of being rejected by the Gascon Regiment itself ("There is nowhere lower to apply") before rising to captain in thirty days — pried open a red-marked crate in a smugglers' cove and took the booby-trapped charge through the chest. The ball flew on, and Pierre d'Amboise — the ruined earldom's scholar, press-ganged into the Frontier Regiment by a helpful stranger's directions ("There it is, sonny, there's the University!") and by spring a private of the Royal Foot Guards, at that moment being frog-marched to the line for attempted desertion (he had recognized the battle's exact resemblance to "General Antonius during the Sicilian Revolt of 268" and tried to go warn the general staff) — was shoved forward "just as that same random cannonball sails into the space just then occupied by his head, and removes it."

> *"The battle continues much as it had before."* — the Narrator, closing the scene

**Anton Dechets, 1612–1628** — *even in death a holy man.* **Pierre d'Amboise, 1609–1628** — *great men require courage and luck. Pierre had courage.*

> 🎲 **At the table.** *"This campaign has been a slaughter,"* wrote one player, and the table's dark-jewel aphorisms poured out: *"#campaign is just the waiting room for #cemetery."* Marius's player had typed *"@Dice Golem has been trying to kill me for years"* minutes before the roll; the bot answered him with a literal error message, then killed him. Sébastien's — that is, Jean's — player had pre-written his battle narration before learning how bad his odds actually were — "so I just ran with it" — and defended the whole ethic of the table in one line: *"What's the point of playing a game with dice, if you make it mathematically impossible for them to matter?"* And Pierre d'Amboise's player, having buried his third character in fourteen months, rolled the loot dice his dead man would have earned — boxcars, naturally — and captioned them: *"The loot that could have been."* Four tombstones went up in five minutes, the biggest single posting since Summer 1624. The month's official announcement reads, in full: "The month in Paris passes without incident."

## III. New men for the last act (May–July)

The graveyard generation was replaced within weeks by the game's final cohort, and they arrived swinging.

From the wreckage of the Soucy line — six characters dead by blade, plank, cannonball, and worse — came the seventh and last: **Abelard Soucy**, son of the Earl of Périgord, an avid horseman fresh from his tutors at the Château de Commarque, "chomping at the bit" to bloom in society as his flourishing older brother had. From Joseph Bousain's three graves came the fourth and final bearer of that line: **Joseph Bousain**, a bastard born to thunder at the stroke of midnight on 6/6/1606, whose adoptive father's cattle had been mysteriously spared from plague, and whose true father he suspected to be "someone very high indeed, or perhaps very, very low" — a colossus with the toughest body ever rolled in the game. From Ghôst Touchard's four came the threadbare **Côme Touchard**, private of the Picardy Musketeers, whose entire manifesto was a toast: *"To being unemployed and unattached in Paris!"* And from the ashes of Kaneda, Bonaparte, Lefebvre, and Shawinigan came the last and loudest: **Albert Blastagarde**, second son of a gunpowder-trading dynasty whose "poorly thought out experiments sometimes thin their ranks," arrived to lend France his explosives expertise. He inspected the recruitment musket with contempt, improved it on the spot with a pocket toolkit, and bought the captaincy of the Picardy Musketeers' F Company — *"The F is for Firepower."*

Even the courtships of that summer had an end-of-days glow. Ghôst Touchard's new man needed a natural six for the eternal Adélaïde Blaise, failed once ("Back to the ol' bawdyhouse for me!"), then rolled it — and the Narrator, writing for a lady now on her *eighth* recorded lover across five years, produced the most quietly uncanny passage in the whole record:

> *"'Déjà vu...' she mutters, before distractedly agreeing to whatever romantic scheme you'd dreamt up this time. She cannot stop staring, but doesn't quite seem to be staring at you."*

She was staring, of course, at the dice.

## IV. The seawall (August)

In the summer, England made its greatest throw: the **Earl of Lindsey** with twenty-nine warships and thirty-one merchantmen — "the largest naval assault ever conducted against the French homeland" — to break the siege of La Rochelle. Against him stood the Cardinal's mole, the great seawall closing the harbor's mouth, and Richelieu in person, who had "consecrated every stone."

![The siege of La Rochelle](images/campaign/campaign_10_1024px-Siege_of_La_Rochelle_1881_Henri_Motte.png)

The battle of the seawall was chaos in brine. The Cardinal's pious but militarily innocent dispositions let the English concentrate on a single section; one brigadier's apparently craven evacuation of his wall was half-vindicated when the unsettled mortar nearly swallowed the English landing whole; another general, dimly recalling something about "gorilla tactics," rode his command into the woods, "which he imagines is where a gorilla would prefer to fight." And Jules Lavelle — Baron Lavelle, the man whose sixes had become a load-bearing feature of the French war effort — watched his commander flee, charged after the English in a fury, boarded a warship at the head of the Royal Foot Guards, and rolled a one. The Guards were butchered on the deck by seasoned sea dogs; the legend of the Lavelle six ended in the scuppers of an English hull.

The English withdrew to regroup; the Cardinal declared an enormous success; and from the doomed city, across the water, came cries of despair. Slade Imbee, nursing a musket wound, received a steely handshake from Richelieu himself and a purse of four hundred crowns: *"Remember his Eminence's generosity, and our great victory."*

## V. The fall of La Rochelle (October–December)

The city starved out at last in the autumn. The great siege — the war's center of gravity since 1627 — cracked open, and the army's final task was not battle but *filtration*: fleeing English commanders and traitor nobles were hiding among the refugee columns, and the regiments drew checkpoint duty.

It was the strangest campaign of the war. Curtis Sinnoch's apocalyptic mobilization speech (*"With each musket shot, LET THUNDER BELLOW! With each sabre thrust, LET LIGHTNING STRIKE!"*) deflated into a week of processing paperwork; his dragnet netted "a few coins of English currency and a single English soldier in a dress doing his best French accent." English infiltrators slit Foot Guard throats on night watch — after the second wine ration — and a heavy-handed colonel's searches sparked riots that ended in his court-martial. And one major, investigating a suspiciously lumpy cabbage cart, was answered by a gang of wanted criminals bursting from the produce "swinging wickedly curved blades in a vortex of leafy death."

Private Côme Touchard fled that vortex successfully — he had prudently arranged to be a coward that day — and survived the battle entirely. Afterward, strolling the wreckage, he paused to console the greengrocer, who stood staring at his bloody, dismembered produce, then turned on Côme with a hiss that has entered the language:

> *"MY CABBAGESSSSSS!"*

The enraged vendor went for his throat. Côme fought him off, lost "a few bits of skin," applied a dirty bandage, and thought no more of it. The wounds turned gangrenous; he died in a hospital tent days later — having, as promised, died for his country, in the sense that the country contained the cabbage merchant who bit him. **Côme Touchard, 1611–1628** — *lived and died by the skin of his teeth.*

The war in Europe closed on a rose-scented note from the Cardinal: *"Never again will the shores of France be sullied by the presence of those who do not worship Christ in accordance with the doctrine of the Pope in Rome."* With the Picardy Musketeers' command staff nearly annihilated, two of the newest captains — Armand Dufour and Albert Blastagarde — found themselves acting battalion commanders. And Curtis Sinnoch, ruling on a fat collaborator merchant's offer of hidden riches for his life — the man led Curtis's retinue to a hollowed tree spilling hundreds of gold coins — delivered the judgment of a man who had been Minister of Justice slightly too long:

> *"This is traitor's money; it belongs to the crown. It is not yours to give."*

His adjutant slit the man's throat. The crown's auditors awarded the Brigadier-General a fifty-crown finder's bonus, and the permanence of his stars.

> 🎲 **At the table.** The machinery beneath the year's honors deserves its own memorial. In the government channel — by 1628 the survivors were playing for *cabinet ministries* — Curtis retained the Ministry of Justice by burning a Level 7 favour for +1 on the die, while Jules missed Commissioner of Public Safety by exactly one pip. In the regimental channels, the post-campaign ritual was now naked: *"So did any colonels die?"* — *"Haven't resolved the NPCs yet."* And the Narrator, closing October 1628 in real-world March 2022, signed the announcement with a wink at the game's magnificent calendar drift: **"The rest of the month passes without incident. Happy Halloween!"**

December was gentle, for once, and the chronicle lingers on it the way the survivors did. At Red Phillip's, three members converged at once ("the barkeeper is ecstatic about tripling his usual weekly sales"): Armand Dufour with the fetching Lady Armadine on his arm — the latest in her long line of recorded suitors — and Albert Blastagarde in a brandy-fueled corner booth, littering the table with weapon designs "ranging from mundane firearms to contraptions which most would dismiss as the product of a madman," including an empty metal barrel on a swivel attached to four wire-laden kites, entitled *Storm Cannon*. Joseph Bousain rode to the ruined Beauchamp estate mounted on Angélique's own beloved horse, freshly repurchased, and offered her the reins — *"Your steed has come back to you"* — winning with a six the lady whose losses no gift could repair, "merely proving that you, at least, see the value of what she has lost." And at Bothwell's Christmas party, under rare Indian orchids and over a 1558 Bordeaux from vines planted by Pope Clement V, the two oldest friends in Paris performed the rite of their tribe one last time:

> *"A fairly good wine, wouldn't you say? Not quite to my taste. Somewhat smacks of verdure, if you ask me."* — Baron Jules Lavelle
>
> *"I'm getting mostly early Merlot in the nose. Frankly, I'm more appreciative of these lovely flowers!"* — the Marquis de Sinnoch

Outside, in the snow, the Duke of Rohan was marching the last Huguenot army in France toward the south. One more winter would settle everything.
