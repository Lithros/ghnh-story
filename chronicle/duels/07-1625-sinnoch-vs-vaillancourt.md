# Curtis Sinnoch vs. Lucien Vaillancourt

*March 1625 · the park by Mme. Brugière's apartments, Paris · Cause: rival courtship of the widow Félice Brugière · sabre against rapier*

The seventh duel of the game, and the shortest decisive one yet: seven turns, one cut, and a clavicle in splinters. It began in the sky — a flock of trained pigeons spelling a lady's name, and a falcon that ate one of the letters. Two months earlier Curtis Sinnoch had lost a duel, a lady, and the use of his shoulder to Alphonse Devereux's elephant; now a newcomer to Paris named Lucien Vaillancourt — the third character of a player whose first two had already died — walked into Curtis's courtship of the most eligible widow in France. Curtis broke *his* shoulder for symmetry, won the duel, and then, four minutes later, won the widow with a single throw of the die. And the widow, it turned out, may have arranged the whole morning.

## The quarrel

### The most eligible widow in Paris

*The prize was created in January 1625, by way of the executioner:*

**Narrator** — "Félice Brugière's husband is executed for high treason. She is now eligible for courting."

**Jules Lavelle** — "It's treason. Mourning a traitor would be highly suspect."

*In February, a new face arrived on the Champs-Élysées and announced his program for living:*

**Lucien Vaillancourt** — "Gentlemen! I am here to drink, flirt, and fight, because only the good Lord knows when we will die!"

**Jules Lavelle** — "From the sound of things, some men are all too eager to take foolish risks and thereby surprise even God with their untimely demise."

**Lucien Vaillancourt** — "This is Paris. There is a never ending stream of fools coming and going. Just ask the lonely ladies!"

### The pigeons and the falcon

*March 1625. The newcomer made straight for the widow — and the Narrator set the scene that caused the duel:*

**Narrator** — "Félice Brugière's newfound status as Paris's most eligible widow is the talk of the town. Though her dead husband disgraced his family, she is too intelligent and well-connected to let that keep her out of polite society for long. It is whispered in dark corners that she has sufficient clout that she was able to reject the King himself.

Lucien Vaillancourt, new to Paris, has little impression of Mme. Brugière's chequered past--he is much too interested in what she can represent for his future. He greets her in the park near her apartments one Sunday morning with a flock of pigeons that have been trained to fly formations spelling the letters of her name.

Mme. Brugière nods with appreciation as her retinue claps at the sight. Suddenly, there is a flash of red, white, and brown in the sky, and the formation is missing a member. The crowd gasps, and Mme. Brugière waves a hand in the direction of Curtis Sinnoch, who wears a glove upon which sits a falcon, viciously tearing into the meat of the slain dove.

Mme. Brugière smiles. 'Some sport, then, messieurs?'"

*Lucien answered the falconry in two registers — first the flourish, then the footnote:*

**Lucien Vaillancourt** — "I am in a sporting mood. Perhaps Monsieur Sinnoch with his microscopic hawk would care to settle this issue before I begin my own peregrination with Mme. Brugière?"

**Lucien Vaillancourt** — "Nice bird, asshole."

*The next morning, Curtis replied in kind, and the bird-pun arms race escalated to steel:*

**Curtis Sinnoch** — "Lucien Vaillancourt I know not who you are, but I appreciate the squab snack you provided for Archimedes here. The most sport I would get out of the likes of you would be continuing to pluck your droll pigeons out of the sky, one by one."

**Lucien Vaillancourt** — "While you seek to feather your nest with Mlle. Brugière's favors, all I see is the albatross around your neck! Remember, the early bird gets the worm."

**Curtis Sinnoch** — "Yes, indeed - and if you had arrived in Paris earlier, maybe your worm would have gotten something - instead, it shall be blood, should you seek it"

*Behind the screen, the odds were reckoned: Lucien, though new to town, "has been practicing" — he held the Expertise advantage (the Narrator's first ruling gave Curtis two extra mandatory rests per sequence, then corrected itself: advantage to Lucien, but no mandatory rests for Curtis). It was the first time in three characters that Lucien's player had ever held the advantage in a duel.*

## The duel

*The gentlemen armed themselves in full view of the birds that had started it all:*

**Narrator** — "when are you two ready to start?"

**Curtis Sinnoch** — "*Curtis lets loose Archimedes once more to snatch a Dove from the air, as he readies for a duel*"

**Lucien Vaillancourt** — "*Lucien dual-wields pidgeons ready for battle*"

**Curtis Sinnoch** — "*Curtis brandishes his former regimental weapon, a Sabre*"

**Lucien Vaillancourt** — "*Lucien produces his rapier*"

**Narrator** — "THE DUEL BEGINS"

### Turn One

**Lucien Vaillancourt** — "I REST."

**Curtis Sinnoch** — "I REST"

### Turn Two

**Lucien Vaillancourt** — "I lunge!"

**Curtis Sinnoch** — "I SLASH"

**Narrator** — "The gentlemen cross blades as Lucien leaps forward, a dozen coruscating doves forming a slow-motion halo in the background. Curtis meets him in the middle with a vicious strike, but each man's blade finds a home in the flesh of the other."

**Narrator** — "Curtis takes 12 damage. Lucien takes 30 damage."

**Jules Lavelle** — "Show him what for, Sinnoch! *\hoots**"

### Turn Three

**Lucien Vaillancourt** — "I REST." — *and, out of the side of his mouth:* "I'm going to get nailed next turn.. ugh"

**Curtis Sinnoch** — "I REST"

### Turn Four

**Lucien Vaillancourt** — "I REST."

**Curtis Sinnoch** — "I CUT"

**Narrator** — "Curtis sees his chance and drives his blade down as though he were astride his charger. There is a sickening crunch as Lucien's skeleton bears the brunt of the blow, and splinters of clavicle spatter the aghast audience."

**Narrator** — "Lucien takes 60 damage."

**Hercule Bonaparte** — "Less a duel than an execution it seems."

### Turn Five

**Lucien Vaillancourt** — "I SLASH."

**Curtis Sinnoch** — "I REST"

**Narrator** — "Lucien summons what strength remains for a desperate grazing action, like unto a starving cow."

**Narrator** — "Curtis takes 12 damage."

**Jules Lavelle** — "That's the way, Sinnoch! Show him your chin!"

### Turn Six

**Lucien Vaillancourt** — "I REST."

**Curtis Sinnoch** — "I REST"

*Lucien's endurance had fallen below half; the rules now demanded mandatory rests of him too.*

**Narrator** — "Please wait while Lucien submits through turn 18"

### Turn Seven

**Lucien Vaillancourt** — "I Lunge!"

**Curtis Sinnoch** — "I REST"

**Narrator** — "Lucien attempts to turn the tide, suddenly pivoting. Though he can barely stand, the doves flutter around him, as though guiding his blade into Curtis's unprotected flank."

**Narrator** — "Curtis takes 24 damage."

**Lucien Vaillancourt** — "I SURRENDER!"

**Narrator** — "Curtis is the winner!"

## The issue

**Narrator** — "Curtis, gain 1 base Expertise and no status, as Lucien is not in a regiment."

**Curtis Sinnoch** — "*Curtis stands, swaying slightly, but having taken less damage then the single blow to Lucien's shoulder*"

**Curtis Sinnoch** — "...Pathetic. You slash a man, then immediately surrender. You made the right choice - leave with your tail between your legs, while you still stand"

*The victor's contempt was, by the numbers, mercy — as both players immediately acknowledged out of character:*

**Ghôst Touchard** — "(Just to note, I was down to 30 hp haha)"

**Curtis Sinnoch** — "(OOC - the next hit would have killed you!)"

**Ghôst Touchard** — "(agreed!)" — "(I submitted all Blocks and a Rest with the intent to surrender)"

**Curtis Sinnoch** — "(Gotta take those mandatory rests! Yeah... I realized the Cut was brutal)"

**Ghôst Touchard** — "(The cut was half my total hp)"

**The reckoning:** Lucien Vaillancourt — surrendered on Turn Seven with 90 of 120 endurance gone, 60 of it to a single cut that shattered his clavicle and showered the spectators with bone; no status forfeit, as he belonged to no regiment. Curtis Sinnoch — 48 endurance lost across three wounds, +1 base Expertise, and the field.

### The courtship, won on the spot

*Four minutes after the surrender, the Narrator turned back to the business the falcon had interrupted:*

**Narrator** — "Curtis Sinnoch, roll to court Félice Brugière (SL 13). With your extravagance, you should be aiming for a 2."

> 🎲 Curtis Sinnoch rolls 1d6 — **6** (courting Félice Brugière, SL 13; target 2 with extravagance: a triumph)

**Narrator** — "The lady is quite taken with M. Sinnoch, and eventually lets herself be taken by him. Yet, one cannot escape the feeling that this was precisely the outcome she intended...

Adélaïde Blaise, alone in her apartments that night, receives a note that reads:

'One man for another. -F'"

*So the widow who "was able to reject the King himself" invited the sport, watched one suitor break the other, took the winner — and sent a cipher into the night. What "One man for another" purchased was never explained to the table.*

### Blood in the street

*That evening, in the streets, the survivors compared notes:*

**Pierre de Tête** — "I missed the fight, but couldn't miss the blood spatters in the street. Curtis Sinnoch, I hope you'll fill me in on the details when we meet at club later this month!"

**Curtis Sinnoch** — "Pierre de Tête, I would be happy to! I don't know who had a better time of it, myself or Archimedes! Ah, Lucien Vaillancourt - I trust next time you may have a bit more discretion? I fear I almost laid you low!"

**Lucien Vaillancourt** — "Curtis Sinnoch, hopefully you are more delicate with the lady!"

*The shattered clavicle healed enough for Lucien to keep drinking, flirting, and fighting, exactly as advertised — but not for long. Nine game-months later, in the winter siege of 1625, he elected "reckless bravery for -1," rolled boxcars on his death roll, and — the Narrator having just declared the fort taken "without suffering a single casualty" — died showing off: "emboldened by the ease of the advance, Lucien decides to show off by climbing the fort's exterior. At the top, he chooses for a handhold a plank that turns out to be rotten, and falls to his death below." His player's farewell for him serves as epitaph:*

**Lucien Vaillancourt** — "I am a bastard, a corpse, but not a coward. for what that's worth"
