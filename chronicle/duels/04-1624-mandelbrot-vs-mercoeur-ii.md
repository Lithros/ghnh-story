# Leon Mandelbrot vs. Etienne de Mercoeur — The Rematch

*May 1624 · the doorstep of Laurence Caillat, Paris · Cause: rival suitors for the same lady — again · rapier against rapier*

The campaign's first rematch, and its players knew it: "the rematch of the century," Etienne's player called it before a blade was drawn. Two months after Leon Mandelbrot and Etienne de Mercoeur bled each other white beneath Adélaïde Blaise's window — and became fast friends over the wounds — the two men arrived, gifts in hand, at the door of the same woman: Laurence Caillat, at social level 11 the most exalted mistress in Paris. This time the whole city had learned to treat a Mandelbrot–Mercoeur duel as scheduled entertainment. There was a spectator's wager of thirty-five crowns riding on the outcome, a side bet on the lady's heart, a fainting mademoiselle at the window, and — at the end — an oath sworn on a grandfather.

## The quarrel

*The seed was planted in April, when Etienne — five duel-wounds barely healed — went courting Mlle. Caillat first, and alone:*

**Narrator** — "Etienne de Mercoeur, roll to court Laurence Caillat (SL 11). Paying one extra time, you should be aiming for a 3."

**Etienne de Mercoeur** — "May fortune be on my side!"

> 🎲 Etienne de Mercoeur rolls 1d6 — **2** (needed 3: rejected)

**Narrator** — "The lady holds out hope for a suitor who has not of late been bested in a duel."

*The wound Leon gave him in March had cost him the lady in April. So in May, both men paid their court — and the Narrator set the scene with what may be the finest single stage-direction of the young campaign:*

**Narrator** — "Leon Mandelbrot and Etienne de Mercoeur, happening to pass one another in the street, share a friendly greeting and make plans to carouse together at Hunter's later in the month. They part amiably to go their own way. Shortly thereafter, they realize they're walking in the same direction. Laughing at the awkwardness, they continue on in good spirits.

Their spirits darken, and bonhomie evaporates, as they each arrive at the doorstep of Mlle. Caillat. Etienne's servants await with some tastefully arranged bouquets and pastry; Leon has dug deep and arranged for a carriage tour and picnic on the grounds at the Versailles constructions. The carriage driver is heard expressing relief that he has been paid in advance."

*Word travelled fast. In the dueling hall, before either principal had said a word, the betting opened:*

**Phillipe du Kaneda** — "Dare I say, a potential duel is in the making between Leon Mandelbrot and Etienne de Mercoeur. Should it come to pass, it would be ungentlemanly of me to wish my future guest ill will... I've got 35 crowns on my man Leon Mandelbrot to the first neutral party interested in the wager."

**Jean-Luc Aveline** — "I said I would refrain from gambling this month, but I cannot resist a good bet. I shall take you up on that, should the duel come to pass!"

**Leon Mandelbrot** — "Etienne de Mercoeur The fates are truly against us these days!"

**Phillipe du Kaneda** — "Alright Leon Mandelbrot, I know its only the first week of May, but for some reason I have this terrible feeling some bad news is coming my way. So if you would kindly kick his ass, I would greatly appreciated it."

**Phillipe du Kaneda** — "My apologizes. My coarse upbringing is showing through."

**Etienne de Mercoeur** — "*sigh* This is truly becoming very vexing, Leon. I don't wish to fight again, but I have little other choice. My life grows colder without a companion."

**Pain Loafmann** — "Ah the monthly duel. *checks the sun* Honestly I'm not sure what I'd do without such a regular spectacle to attend."

*Aveline, not content with thirty-five crowns on the line, tried to sweeten the pot with a death clause:*

**Jean-Luc Aveline** — "Phillipe du Kaneda, what say you to an additional 5 crowns if our champion slays their rival (fatally)? That would sort out the issue of these duels. I would not be surprised if the conflict was sorted amicably, however."

**Phillipe du Kaneda** — "Jean-Luc Aveline While I find your proposal intriguing, I'm going to politely decline the additional wager. Simply, an outcome of that magnitude deserves a proportionate wager. One which is outside my reach the month."

**Leon Mandelbrot** — "Etienne de Mercoeur Unfortunately I feel the same way!"

*In the courting channel, Aveline hedged his position with a second wager — on the lady herself:*

**Jean-Luc Aveline** — "I'd be willing to bet 10 crowns that Mlle. Caillat has found a match by the end of the month regardless of the outcome of the duel!"

*The GM clarified the price of peace: Leon, the lower-placed man, could withdraw without penalty in deference to Etienne's higher station; if Etienne withdrew, he would lose status equal to the difference in their stations — and either way, every crown already spent on the courting was forfeit. ("It's meant to encourage people to duel via sunk cost fallacy," the GM admitted.)*

**Leon Mandelbrot** — "Alas, I cannot afford to withdraw!"

**Godefroy de Beaufosse** — "The anticipation is as palpable as a ripe fig!"

*On the last day of the week the principals met to name their weapons:*

**Leon Mandelbrot** — "Etienne de Mercoeur What is your weapon of choice on this now spoiled day?"

**Etienne de Mercoeur** — "The same as our first meeting, alas - the rapier."

**Leon Mandelbrot** — "Same, I too will be using the gentleman's weapon, the Rapier"

**Jean-Luc Aveline** — "The gentleman's weapon is his tongue"

**Leon Mandelbrot** — "I do fear for the poor soul who gets into an altercation with Pain Loafmann. I understand he has been favoring a two handed weapon"

**Pain Loafmann** — "What this? *hoisting BROTZWEIHANDERBAUERJAGERIN* She is simply a tool of my trade, nothing more."

*In March, Etienne had held the advantage. But Leon's victory then had earned him a point of rapier expertise, and both men now stood exactly equal at 11 — so for the first time in the campaign, the advantage went to a die:*

> 🎲 Leon Mandelbrot rolls 1d6 — **4** · Etienne de Mercoeur rolls 1d6 — **2** (equal expertise 11: Leon has advantage)

## The issue

*Seventeen turns. Etienne de Mercoeur took four wounds of exactly 22 apiece — 88 endurance gone, eleven more than the beating he took in March — while landing only a 9 and an 18 on Leon, 27 in all with the opening trade's 18 making 45. As in March, he yielded on his feet; as in March, the two men could not stop compliments long enough to bleed.*

**Leon Mandelbrot** — "I raise my blade once more in salute, out of breath"

**Narrator** — "Leon breathes heavily, the sweet taste of victory mingled with blood in his mouth."

**Narrator** — "Leon, gain one expertise"

**Leon Mandelbrot** — "You are a most worthy opponent Sir!"

*And then Etienne de Mercoeur — twice-beaten, four times skewered, twice denied the same two ladies by the same man — delivered the most gallant concession of the war:*

**Etienne de Mercoeur** — "And you... as well... And I swear on my grandfather, if you do not win Mlle. Caillat, I will trounce you bare-fisted, bleeding or no."

**Leon Mandelbrot** — "Not if I trounce myself!"

**Phillipe du Kaneda** — "Good show Leon Mandelbrot, good show. Your drinks shall be on me when we visit the Red Phillips later this month. Or should I say, they'll be on Jean-Luc Aveline."

**Leon Mandelbrot** — "I look forward to it!"

**Jean-Luc Aveline** — "I only seek to spread joy around!"

**Phillipe du Kaneda** — "Now, go win that lady's favor!"

**Godefroy de Beaufosse** — "Congratulations to the victor, Leon Mandelbrot"

**Leon Mandelbrot** — "I shall. Untill next week my friends!"

*Minutes later, in the courting channel, the whole affair came down to one die. The March duel had ended exactly here — and the lady of March had rejected the bleeding victor for failing to kill. This time:*

**Narrator** — "Leon Mandelbrot, roll to court Laurence Caillat (SL 11). Having spent lavishly, you should be aiming for a 2."

> 🎲 Leon Mandelbrot rolls 1d6 — **3** (needed 2: the lady is won)

**Jean-Luc Aveline** — "Well won!"

**Leon Mandelbrot** — "THANK YOU Dice Golem"

**Narrator** — "The lady, upon regaining consciousness, swears to be yours forever and a day."

**Leon Mandelbrot** — "My lady, I bled for you this day, and will bleed for you forever more!"

**Leon Mandelbrot** — "But for now, let us enjoy our carriage ride and picnic at the Versailles"

*So the grandfather's honor was safe, and Etienne was spared a bare-fisted trouncing. That evening the wagers were settled in the same hall where they were struck:*

**Jean-Luc Aveline** — "Jean-Luc Aveline pays Phillipe du Kaneda his 35 crowns."

**Phillipe du Kaneda** — "Jean-Luc Aveline My thanks, good sir!"

*Aveline's hedge, at least, paid: Mlle. Caillat had indeed "found a match by the end of the month regardless of the outcome of the duel," and the ten crowns he'd staked on it against Pierre de Tête — wagering by post from the front lines — came home a winner. And the Hunter's engagement made after the March duel was kept that same month, with the loser as host. The war had just claimed Guillaume d'Avergny, the chronicle's first grave, and Etienne poured with one arm:*

**Etienne de Mercoeur** — "/His arm still in a sling, he awkwardly grasps his glass and refrains from accidentally(?) pouring it on Leon at any given time. Aye, the war comes to France, and we must answer the call. It has already taken its first casualty among our numbers."

**Leon Mandelbrot** — "I buy Etienne several drinks"

*The lady's devotion outlasted the season. When the armies marched and Leon marched with them, the Narrator looked in on her — and on the nobleman who tried to profit by the vacancy:*

**Narrator** — "Laurence Caillat stares idly at her windowsill. She has lain there, day after day, awaiting the return of her sweet Leon. As she refuses to move from the spot, the better to show her dedication, she has blocked the servants from their duties, and a layer of dust has built up before her eyes. She watches an ant trace a path through the dust, as lost as she fears her dear heart to be.

She is shocked from her reverie by the vision of a dashing gentleman on the other side of the window. Could it be? Her beloved, returned early from the war?

But no. It is none other than Jules Lavelle, clutching a gorgeous crystal necklace, shaking it at her through the window. He has come courting. And Leon gave her such a nice carriage ride, but nothing she could wear..."

**Narrator** — "The lady remains true; the crystal necklace cannot sway her from her solemn vigil."

**Jules Lavelle** — "Curse your beau and may he find himself on the wrong end of a cannon!"

*The curse missed its man. Leon came home from the August bloodbath a captain; it was Etienne de Mercoeur — the richest, best-born, most gallant of the founding gentlemen — who never returned, cut down on campaign three months after this duel. The rematch was the last time he ever drew a sword against a friend. As for how the whole business looked in hindsight, Leon himself gave the definitive account when Jules asked him, that autumn, how one wins the heart of so refined a lady:*

**Leon Mandelbrot** — "Aside from my roguish charm? A carriage ride, a picnic, a poem, and nearly killing another suitor in front of her."

**The reckoning:** Etienne de Mercoeur — yielded on turn seventeen, 88 endurance lost across four identical wounds of 22, his second defeat to the same blade, redeemed by the campaign's most gallant oath. Leon Mandelbrot — victorious, 45 endurance lost across three wounds, rapier expertise raised to 12, and the hand of Laurence Caillat (SL 11) won on a roll of 3 — "forever and a day." Phillipe du Kaneda — 35 crowns richer. Jean-Luc Aveline — 35 crowns poorer, 10 crowns richer, and self-described spreader of joy.
