# Clemens Charron vs. Jules Lavelle

*November 1624 · Hunter's, Paris — the doors thrown open to the street · Cause: a commoner's public wish "to see more lions freeze" · sabre against sabre*

The fifth duel of the game, and the most elaborately staged: the insulted nobleman answered a peasant's insolence not with a dawn meeting but with a dinner invitation — hosting his own insulter to wine and conversation at Hunter's, his club, before the sabres came out on the club floor. It was also the first duel fought by the Narrator's own character, Major Jules Lavelle of the Royal Foot Guards, so a neutral player was deputized to verify the order sheets. And — unknown to nearly everyone at the table — the muscular peasant sipping the Lavelle estate vintage had been paid, twice, in unmarked pouches of crowns, to put his host in the ground.

*Editorial note: quoted text is verbatim from the game record; Discord @-mentions inside quotations are rendered as the character's name. The paid-hit correspondence below unfolded in the streets channel over the two game-months before the duel.*

## The quarrel

### A necklace, a list, and a pouch of crowns

*The seed was planted back in September 1624, and it had nothing to do with Clemens Charron. Hercule Bonaparte — a stone-faced commoner of growing means — wrote to Jules Lavelle about a certain piece of jewelry the Major had been seen hurling into a park:*

**Hercule Bonaparte** — "Dear Mr. Lavelle, I write to you today on a matter of a return of property. While the Gazette is a buzz with news of a missing necklace, those who pay attention to the word on the street suspect its true ownership has been misattributed. If anything, the scene of a noble born hurling a piece of jewelery as forcefully as he can into a park is a memorable sight to those who behold it. At least, such is what rumors say. Regardless, even a noble one can be forgiven for the odd and brief act of frustration now and then. As it so happens, a servant of mine happened to be passing through said park and noticed something gleaming in the tall grass. It was fortuitous that the sun happened to be at the correct angle for this to occur. It truly is a remarkable and beautiful piece. It deserves to be around the neck of your beau, not lying in a field. I understand you frequent Hunters regularly and would be happy to return it to you there. You need simply name a time and ensure that the host knows that I am your guest. Yours faithfully, Hercule Bonaparte"

**Jules Lavelle** — "Dear M. Bonaparte, When first I received your missive, I do confess I took it for implying the most heinous libel and extortion. On closer examination, I see this was not your intention. In light of my grievous error in misunderstanding your most gentlemanly intent, allow me to invite you to accompany me to Hunter's. For consideration I merely ask you discreetly dispose of said article of jewelry, and never again repeat your discovery to another. Silly, I know, but even the son of Marquis Lavelle is not immune to the vagaries of superstition. Yours in utmost faith and confidence, Maj. Jules Lavelle"

**Hercule Bonaparte** — "Dear Maj. Jules Lavelle, My apologies for any confusion with respect to my previous correspondence. It is my own fault for indulging in the local rumor mill. I should learn that such false rubbish should be beneath me. Consider the matter dust in the wind. I also wish to apologies for forgetting myself and your rank in my previous salutation. I am still growing accustom to Paris traditions. I am honored and humbled by your invitation and would gladly join you, if you were to only name a time. At the very least, I would insist on purchasing your drinks for your hospitality. Yours faithfully, Hercule Bonaparte"

*That same evening, Bonaparte's player posted this into the street channel:*

**Hercule Bonaparte** — "Uncovered during the Paris reconstruction of the 1950s, an untitled notation in the private journal of one Hercule Bonaparte: Curtis Sinnoch / Pain Loafmann / Pierre de Tete / Leon Mandelbrot / Alphonse Devereux / Jules Lavelle / Guy de Cavoye / Clemens Charron / Henri Toulouse"

**Jules Lavelle** — "*Jules shivers, as if someone has just walked over his grave*"

*And a few hours after that, Bonaparte made a stranger an offer:*

**Hercule Bonaparte** — "Hercule Bonaparte approaches you on the street. The weight on his cane is clearly more than for show. Good sir, I know we are but strangers to one another, but there is a business matter I wish to discuss with you at my home in private. To show my good faith, *produces a small pouch* this is 15 Crowns. You may keep it simply for hearing my offer. Without waiting for your reply, Hercule turns his back on you and beings to walk away. Presumably towards his residence. Do you follow to hear him out?"

**Clemens Charron** — "You have my attention. If you're willing to show off more of your coin collection, then you may even get...even more of my attention. Two attentions."

### Tomatoes at the gallows

*October 1624. The rebel leaders of the summer's peasant rising were hanged before city hall, and the whole cast turned out:*

**Narrator** — "A crowd gathers in the square before city hall. The officers of the Cardinal's Guard arrived early in the morning to oversee the workers assembling the gallows. As the morning mists steam off of the cobblestones, the rebel leaders Donat and Barran are marched up onto the platform where they will meet their demise."

**Narrator** — "As he finishes reading the charges, he rolls up the paper and stares over the bloodthirsty crowd. 'Now, it is time for the people to voice their grievances. In the eyes of men and God, feel free to state what these two men have taken from each of you!'"

**Jules Lavelle** — "And here I left my tomatoes at home."

**Leon Mandelbrot** — "Their rampage killed my friends Phillipe du Kaneda and Jean-Luc Aveline!" — "*hands Jules Lavelle several Tomatoes*"

**Jules Lavelle** — "Huzzah! *throws with gusto, missing widely*"

*The trapdoors were sprung; the Narrator pronounced "the safety of France is ensured for one more day"; Jules called it "grisly, but such is the requirement for a functioning civil society." Then a big man in a cheap jacket rounded on him:*

**Clemens Charron** — "Lavelle! You have made it clear before that you think little of the struggle to better the lives of those who occupy a class different from your own. Would you say that executions such as this month's should be standard protocol?"

**Clemens Charron** — "Do you think so little of your countrymen that you would see them all slaughtered with no consideration as to why they fight?"

**Clemens Charron** — "What I'm saying is: who else would you toss tomatoes at before their execution? It's a waste of highly valuable foodstuffs."

**Jules Lavelle** — "You would not expect buzzing insects on the street this late in the year, but here we are."

**Jules Lavelle** — "My family donates generously to manifold breadlines. I'd say that earns me license to dispose of an errant overripe vegetable, even if it is a valuable New World import."

**Clemens Charron** — "You and your less fortunate countymen have something in common, Lavelle - all you have is your family. Without that, what are you left with? That family's grandiose bread donations are but an afterthought by those with little care where their coin travels, as long as it buys them the right to spit upon those they see beneath them."

**Clemens Charron** — "I have known many like you and your family, and each has been more oleaginous than the last. It is a wonder they do not melt into greasy pools when it rains."

**Clemens Charron** — "And let me tell you that a rain shall come, my dear Lavelle."

*And here Jules made the move that shaped everything to follow — not a challenge, but an invitation:*

**Jules Lavelle** — "Very amusing, fellow. If I weren't so sure of your addled mind, I might even take offense! Instead, I shall take pity. Come see me at Hunter's the third week of November. Let us hash this all out over drinks!"

**Jules Lavelle** — "My treat"

**Clemens Charron** — "I assure you that I am of sound mind, and for that reason will consider your invitation."

*Two days later, the first payment arrived:*

**Hercule Bonaparte** — "When Clemens Charron returns home there is a small pouch containing 50 Crowns awaiting him. This is no note nor any other identifier of the origin of the plain brown pouch (OOC Mechanics: Hercule transfers 50 Crowns to Clemens)"

**Clemens Charron** — "What a strange thing to find. Better put it to good use."

### Lions and fawns

*Late October. Jules — perpetually short of funds — went begging in the street, and Clemens saw his opening:*

**Jules Lavelle** — "Could anyone advance me a small loan? Just a couple hundred crowns, a pittance to this esteeméd assemblage to be sure?"

**Clemens Charron** — "Perhaps, my most accomplished Mr. Lavelle, you could attempt the most edifying experience of living without great wealth for a short month."

**Jules Lavelle** — "Good sir. A man of my position in society cannot be seen in want. Does not the cold freeze the weakest first? When you spy a lion a-shiver, show concern for the fawn. Keep the lions warm, and the fawn shall prosper."

**Clemens Charron** — "Ah, but we know that the fawn may seek shelter with its fellow deer, while a lion among fawns seeks naught but a meal. That's said back where I come from, it is."

**Jules Lavelle** — "As for you, M. Charron, it is as natural for the lion to eat the fawn as for the fawn to eat the grass. But none are meant to freeze."

**Clemens Charron** — "If none are meant to freeze then why do we face the cold winds and ice each year, without fail? This would appear to be just as natural, if not more so. It is through our own concerted human effort that we confront this seemingly natural order, and ensure that both frigid weather and rogue lions are kept in check."

**Jules Lavelle** — "You were the one who implied the frigidity would be edifying, M. Charron. I merely mean to say that society functions best when the fawn does not see the lion in danger. What threatens the lion surely endangers the fawn manifold."

**Clemens Charron** — "I will be as clear as ice, my dear Lavelle: I do hope to see more lions freeze, for they have gone untroubled for so long."

**Jules Lavelle** — "Is this man opining I were better dead? Fellows in the street, what say you? If so, such a graceless insult it would be, after my generous invitation to host him at my club. He seems to me to be a man of good sense. I cannot think that is what he meant. But if another confirms his meaning to me, I suppose I will have no recourse."

**Clemens Charron** — "This man may believe what he likes. I would trust the wisdom of the people as to whether his offense is justified - or whether my honor is at stake."

*The street obliged. Curtis Sinnoch, away at the front, could only flex from a distance ("/wishes to join in, but is a Man away at war, unlike the Boys at home playing make-believe/"). Pierre de Tête weighed in — and so, with exquisite cynicism, did the man who was paying for the whole show:*

**Pierre de Tête** — "Dear me! Now I'm not one to foolishly jump into a duel at an insignificant provocation but this seems to be an honest expression of ill intent (especially towards those in higher ranks to which I aspire). I dare say it shall be a very cold winter if we all spoke this way."

**Hercule Bonaparte** — "*muttering to himself in earshot of Jules Lavelle* A peasant publicly ridicules a Major and gets invited to dinner. After accepting the invitation, the same peasant publicly ridicules the Major's position in our great society and muses that his death would pose a benefit. I don't know what to make of this, surely how the Major defends his honor will speak to how he defends France."

**Jules Lavelle** — "You are quite right. If commoners may speak thus to the crème of society, what next? It is my duty to no longer make nice."

**Jules Lavelle** — "Clemens Charron, your low character speaks for itself. A son of Lavelle cannot permit such an insult to pass. I challenge you to a duel! Why not join me at Hunter's the third week of November, and I will treat you -- only not to their fine libations, but to a taste of my blade."

**Pierre de Tête** — "We can all see M. Charron's jacket barely constrains his muscles but it seems HE can barely constrain his Croquant ideology."

**Jules Lavelle** — "Hmm? Did he mention salt taxes?"

**Pierre de Tête** — "I'm sure I've overheard him...."

**Clemens Charron** — "Jules Lavelle Blades first, libations second! I accept."

**Clemens Charron** — "I assure you I am worth my salt."

*Paris settled in to watch:*

**Leon Mandelbrot** — "I may not be able to enter Hunters, but I for one wish to at the least observe the mettle of these two relative newcomers to Paris"

**Guy de Cavoye** — "Take copious notes for when I return to Paris, although I have no doubt the Gazette will cover this fracas."

### Dinner at Hunter's

*The third week of November 1624. An hour before the appointed dinner, Clemens stopped by Mlle. Suzanne LeBeau's apartments to flex at her — "Ah, my fair lady. Could I be as lucky to spend even a moment in your presence? *flexes*":*

> 🎲 Clemens Charron rolls 1d6 — **3** (courting Suzanne LeBeau, SL 7; target 5: rejected)

**Narrator** — "The lady begins to laugh, then realizes Clemens wasn't kidding. She has the grace to blush before letting him down gently."

**Clemens Charron** — "like she had anything better to do this evening..."

*Then, to the club, where the host was already seated:*

**Jules Lavelle** — "Here we are, my friends, right this way. I shall be having a fine meal, maybe my last, before that ox Clemens arrives to spoil my appetite with his low manners."

**Jules Lavelle** — "It may be chilly, but let's open the doors to the street. It wouldn't do for the people of Paris to miss out on a thrashing."

**Hercule Bonaparte** — "I did not know the proprietors would allow matters of honor to be settled on their grounds."

**Jules Lavelle** — "My good fellow, Clemens will be here as my guest. If we should get into an altercation, the damages will be covered by my dues. Though, do be sure to take the bottles away when the fight commences. Wasting wine would be a worse disgrace than refusing to fight."

**Hercule Bonaparte** — "Of course. Such is the responsibility one takes when one extends guest rights. Though I suppose we shall be forgoing the right of protection under these circumstances..."

**Jules Lavelle** — "A gentleman's honor takes precedence, I'm afraid."

*The guest of honor arrived:*

**Clemens Charron** — "Heeeey my dudes! This place is red-velvet-fancy."

**Jules Lavelle** — "Ah, yes. Try not to get mud on anything."

**Pierre de Tête** — "But surely a majority would fit your disposition...ahhh, soft, the challenger approaches"

**Clemens Charron** — "The barkeep was a tad surly, though."

**Jules Lavelle** — "That may be because he speaks French."

**Clemens Charron** — "Oh, strange."

**Jules Lavelle** — "I myself have found that condition quite stressful when it comes to relating to you, Clemens."

**Clemens Charron** — "It's only my passion that inflames those around me."

**Pierre de Tête** — "Mlle. LeBeau didn't seem too inflamed..."

**Clemens Charron** — "that...is purely her loss."

**Jules Lavelle** — "My, and here I thought I was to be the one murdered today. Nevertheless! I hear liquor inflames nicely as well. Please, help yourself to a drink, or eight, before we...get started."

*What followed was the finest passive-aggressive wine tasting of the campaign:*

**Clemens Charron** — "Ah, but what do you call this beverage? It resembles the long-aged liquors we would make at home as children."

**Jules Lavelle** — "Ah, so we have something in common! We produced our own vintage from our estate, as well. In fact, I think there may be a few bottles in the cellar...shall I order one brought up?"

**Pierre de Tête** — "*from the street* Oui!"

**Narrator** — "The staff move to shut the doors, but Jules waves them away. They settle back into their waiting, extremely disgruntled."

**Clemens Charron** — "Why of course! This is bringing back memories. I vividly recall mucking out barns and foraging for forest delicacies. How is it that your estate has captured such a feeling?"

**Jules Lavelle** — "If you do not like the wine, sir, you need only say so. De gustibus non est disputandum, as they say."

**Clemens Charron** — "Let me not be misunderstood, my dear Lavelle - these are compliments! I am enjoying myself most heartily."

**Jules Lavelle** — "By all means, then. You must be quite the sommelier. I had not...detected those notes, myself. But I grew up with the stuff. Fish have trouble describing the water, of course."

**Clemens Charron** — "Ah, but the fish didn't have to make it, nor convince anyone else to drink it!"

**Jules Lavelle** — "Well. To be quite clear, I didn't make it, of course. It was a product of our estate. It wasn't as though I was picking the grapes."

**Clemens Charron** — "Someone certainly did! What do the people responsible for the picking think of your fine wines? Do they enjoy the flavor?"

**Jules Lavelle** — "How on earth would I know that?"

**Clemens Charron** — "Why, the French language, the same as the barkeep of this establishment apparently speaks!"

**Jules Lavelle** — "Oh, our vineyard workers were mostly poor English working off their prison debts. I don't speak a word of their Germanic tongue."

**Clemens Charron** — "In that case I certainly see the logic. Never met an Englishman worth a damn."

**Jules Lavelle** — "I think I've had my fill. Unless you are prepared to retract your insult, it is time we settled matters."

**Clemens Charron** — "No retraction! Let us have at it!"

**Narrator** — "Jules has the advantage."

**Clemens Charron** — "Then I, too, shall use the sabre."

**Narrator** — "Clemens Charron, please submit orders as a list of Routines adding up to at least 12 turns' worth of actions. Jules Lavelle, at least 6."

## The issue

**Narrator** — "Clemens Charron, as winner, gain 1 base Expertise"

**Clemens Charron** — "Well! That was some refreshing sport. I appreciate such a spirited duel, my good Lavelle!"

**Hercule Bonaparte** — "Hercule remains stone faced throughout the duel. If he had any opinion of this result it does not register"

**Jules Lavelle** — "I wish you...bleed...all the best..."

**The reckoning:** Jules Lavelle — surrendered on Turn Twelve, 45 endurance lost to three identical 15-point lunges; every attack Clemens landed in the duel was the same thrust, and Jules blocked against the slash every time. Clemens Charron — 14 endurance lost (a chin wound "which barely registers"), +1 base Expertise, and — see below — 100 crowns in unmarked pouches.

*Back inside the club, the party resumed as though the bloodletting had been the dessert course:*

**Jules Lavelle** — "You are a strong man, Clemens, willing to fight for your beliefs. France needs more of that! *wheeze*"

**Pierre de Tête** — "Well fought, gentlemen!"

**Clemens Charron** — "And you, Jules - I hope to see more of your family's wines flowing in the future, and wish you a sound recovery!"

**Narrator** — "The maître d', grateful that the bulk of the blood was drawn outside, begins to intone a testament to the duel: 'Yea, came here two to battle, one David, one Goliath; one Giant, one Middling; but which was brought low, and which raised up? Perhaps that is not for men to know.'"

**Narrator** — "One of the waiters takes him tenderly by the hand and leads the addled old server back inside."

**Jules Lavelle** — "*stares at his sabre's point, and gingerly taps his finger to it, then pulls it back and sucks on the bleeding fingertip*"

### The revelation

*The next day, out of character, Hercule Bonaparte's player announced a status ding to Social Level 6 — and then explained where the momentum was coming from. The dinner-party duel had been a purchased hit all along:*

**WillWorker** — "OC: It helps when the man you paid to have beat/killed over a minor slight invites you to dinner."

**WillWorker** (to the Narrator, whose character had just been thrice skewered) — "OC: Clemens let you off. I had offered him another 50 Crowns to simply throw a dagger at you on the 50/50 he would do 90 to 120 damage."

**Jules Lavelle** — "what a good guy...."

**harry** — "Yeah that would have been fun"

**harry** — "kinda felt wrong after the drinks n' conversation"

*So the record shows: Hercule Bonaparte — the man whose necklace letter Jules had once mistaken for "the most heinous libel and extortion," the man who declared the matter "dust in the wind," the man who goaded Jules into the challenge in earshot on the street, the man who sat stone-faced through the whole duel as an invited guest — had paid Clemens Charron in anonymous pouches to beat or kill his host, with a bonus on offer for a thrown dagger that stood even odds of dealing 90 to 120 damage. Clemens took the beating money, declined the killing bonus, and spared the man who poured him his childhood's own terrible wine. Jules Lavelle survived his own dinner party by the grace of a hitman's sentimentality.*
