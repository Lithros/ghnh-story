# Robert d'Engaux vs. Pépin Bonheur

*April 1627 · Red Phillips gambling club, Paris · Cause: Mlle. Armadine Demachy — a ring slipped onto her finger mid-date · rapier against rapier*

The twelfth duel of the game, and the second fought at Red Phillips in as many months — the floorboards were still stained from the February afternoon when Henri Toulouse took Guerinet Durand's head off at the moment of his surrender. Guerinet's player came back as Pépin Bonheur, a shabby, chocolate-bearing musketeer, and promptly re-courted the very mistress Guerinet had died holding: Armadine Demachy. Robert d'Engaux, a debt-ridden cavalryman who had already lost one courtship on a roll of 1 and much of his purse at the Red Phillips tables, decided the direct approach was better — he waited until Pépin stepped up to the bar mid-date and went down on one knee with a ring. The duel that followed the next morning was, by the Narrator's own parenthetical testimony, the parry-heaviest ever fought in the game: twenty-two rounds of deflections, ripostes, mirrored feints, and one knee to the jaw that broke Robert's teeth for 33 damage. Pépin surrendered at 13 endurance, vomited, and passed out into the nearest chair — and Robert walked outside, brushed himself off, and won the lady over her lover's unconscious body with a 6.

## The quarrel

### The wooing of Armadine Demachy (March 1627)

*Pépin Bonheur, newly arrived in Paris and newly enlisted in the Picardy Musketeers, announced his intentions in the street — vaguely enough to alarm the wrong Demachy's protector:*

**Pépin Bonheur** — "Ohhhh is there anything greater in all of Paris than the love of a noble woman? I'll be wooing the Beautiful Mme. Demachy at the beginning of the month!"

**Marius Thibodeaux** — "They are both certainly beautiful!"

**Hercule Bonaparte** — "Word of Mr. Bonheur's vague statement reaches Hercule's ears, who reacts with his usual steel-eyed impassiveness. Obviously, Mr. Bonheur means Alida's available and influential sister and not Alida herself. One would offer a potential familial connection into the Knight's favor. The other would be a Captain offering insult to a Noble General Officer of wealth and means. It would only make sense that Mr. Bonheur would seek the path of advancement and not that of effective suicide."

**Pépin Bonheur** — "/emoji for nervously pulling at one's collar/"

*The courtship itself interrupted Hercule and Alida's dinner. The Narrator asked the necessary question first:*

**Narrator** — "Pépin Bonheur, which A. Demachy are you courting?"

**Pépin Bonheur** — "Armadine, beautiful Armadine! It is I, Pepin Bonheur, come to woo you with my heart full of love. I've brought chocolate!"

**Hercule Bonaparte** — "Hercule's steel-eyed gaze betrays him only by the slightly longer pause than would be normal for him. Alida takes note — Why, I believe this is Pepin Bonheur? One of the newest and finest recruits to the Picardy Musketeers. And with overtures of love, no less. Armadine, you could do much worse than this lad." — *and, to the Narrator:* "If possible, I want to spend the crowns necessary to give Pépin Bonheur a +1 to their roll."

**Narrator** — "The lady's eyes widen; she hadn't realized her suitor was so grandly connected, in spite of his shabby appearance. +1 granted" ... "(12 crowns)"

**Pépin Bonheur** — "*with hat in hand, nervously* Ah, Sir Bonaparte, good evening, sir. I didn't mean to interrupt your meal. You are most kind, sir. I have been training with the Picardy Musketeers for a month now" ... "*bows to Alida, then turns to Armadine and recites a short verse on bended knee*"

**Narrator** — "Pépin, the lady seems most amiably disposed towards you. You need to roll a 2."

> 🎲 Pépin Bonheur rolls 1d6 — **2** (needed 2: won, exactly)

**Narrator** — "The lady is wholly won over, though she keeps glancing back at her sister, seemingly looking for approval. This time, at least, the approval is granted."

**Pépin Bonheur** — "*beaming and scarcely believing his good fortune, Pepin follows the butler's lead and takes a seat across from Armadine. He bears a goofy grin because... He had prepared no further words.*"

### A rival in the shadows

*That same March, another new arrival was watching. Robert d'Engaux had come to Paris, joined Princess Louisa's Light Dragoons, bought a Red Phillips membership, and gone straight into debt at its tables:*

**Robert d'Engaux** — "A downtrodden Robert trudges back from Red Philips, his mind filled with images of the debt collectors who hang over his head. As he approaches his apartment, he notices a lady standing on the street corner. Enraptured by her beauty and emboldened by drink he begins to approach. Fortunately, he notices the lady's companion before he reaches her and darts into his apartment before he is spotted. Inside, he mutters to himself 'Alas, one of us will be dead before this business is over.'"

*The companion in question, oblivious, invited his future opponent out for a drink:*

**Pépin Bonheur** — "Robert d'Engaux, bonjour! ... Although, perhaps not. You don't look so well. Why don't we meet up at Red Phillips in the first week and talk it over a glass of port?"

**Robert d'Engaux** — "I am indisposed at that particular time, monsieur. Perhaps we could meet in the second week?"

**Pépin Bonheur** — "The second week it is!"

*Robert was indeed indisposed the first week of April. He had other plans at Red Phillips.*

### The ring (first week of April 1627)

**Narrator** — "Pépin Bonheur is having a lovely evening with Mlle. Armadine at the club. The drinks flow freely, and the lady is even freer with her compliments for the dashing musketeer. Her promises grow more and more daring, and Pépin finds he can scarcely contain his excitement. He goes up to the bar for one last round of drinks. Arms laden with tall and frosty mugs of ale, he turns and is shocked to witness none but Robert d'Engaux on one knee, slipping a gaudy gem-inset ring onto the lady's finger! She giggles, tipsy enough to have forgotten herself."

**Marius Thibodeaux** — "(Quelle horreur!)"

**Robert d'Engaux** — "Mademoiselle, I have loved you since I set eyes on you. I cannot bear to see you in the hands of this fiend!"

**Pépin Bonheur** — "I'm sorry, sir, you must be confused!" ... "The lady is indeed beautiful but I woo'd her first. Bro rules, and all."

**Narrator** — "The bartender rolls his eyes and begins preparing bandages."

**Marius Thibodeaux** — "There is a faint chant of 'Duel! Duel! Duel!' coming from down the street at the Blue Gables where Marius telepathically knows something is going to go down at Red Phillips tonight."

**Pépin Bonheur** — "*stage whisper* no one needs your inflammatory statements"

**Pépin Bonheur** — "Armadine, look at this rubbish! What would your sister say if she saw you come home wearing that?!"

**Robert d'Engaux** — "Do not listen to this sorry excuse for a man, Armadine, you look wonderful"

**Pépin Bonheur** — "Bartender, call a carriage for Milady. It's a duel, then."

## The duel

*Robert's player was brand new to the dueling tables, so the Narrator ran him through the rules first. With the higher expertise, Robert held the advantage: he would submit only six rounds of routines at a time to Pépin's twelve. Then, the weapons:*

**Robert d'Engaux** — "I summon my manservant to look over my weapons and select my trusty rapier"

**Narrator** — "Pépin Bonheur, your weapon?"

**Pépin Bonheur** — "also the rapier"

**Narrator** — "The duel begins." ... "I will declare each round. Then the duelists declare their action for that round. Then I announce the result."

**Narrator** — "The coachman has arrived to take Mlle. Demachy away, but he himself has decided to stick around to watch. She observes as well, peeking around his shoulder."

## The issue

*The winner had taken 77 damage — a slashed off-arm, twin exchanges of rib-wounds, a muscle-tearing cut, and a jawful of broken teeth — and the loser 64. Neither man finished the fight standing:*

**Robert d'Engaux** — "*Robert slumps into the nearest chair*"

**Narrator** — "For winning the duel, Robert gains +2 SP for the month. Increase your expertise by one, Robert."

**Pépin Bonheur** — "*At 13 endurance, I vomit and pass out into the nearest chair*"

*Two minutes later, the Narrator opened the courting channel:*

**Narrator** — "Robert d'Engaux, the lady awaits you without. With her lover passed out in a chair, and you having handily bested him, now is the perfect moment to capitalize on the fiery passions your duel has stirred up."

**Robert d'Engaux** — "I brush myself off and approach her"

**Narrator** — "Roll to court Armadine Demachy (SL 4). Although she has a lover already, she is agog over the lovely gift of jewelry. You need a 3."

> 🎲 Robert d'Engaux rolls 1d6 — **6** (needed 3: the lady is won — the table reacted: 🎊 💍 😭 😖)

**Narrator** — "She takes your arm and never looks back. You have won her with blood, and such things are thicker than Hercule's approval."

*Hercule Bonaparte, who had paid twelve crowns to install Pépin in Armadine's affections, quietly wrote off his investment:*

**Hercule Bonaparte** — "Over lunch Alida once more raises with Hercule that, now that her sister has a beau, they should go on a double date to Bothwell's. Hercule, his mind preoccupied with next month's campaigning, waves the suggestion off. He assures her that he will happily take her and her sister to Bothwell's when he returns, along with her new beau. Whoever that may be." — *and, out of character:* "(Read: Whoever has Armadine's hand on the third week of June 1627, gets a free toady to Bothwell's)"

**Narrator** — "Making promises during the summer! Daring"

*And the glass of port the two men had scheduled before any of this happened? They kept the appointment. The second week of April, victor and vanquished returned to the scene together:*

**Narrator** — "Tensions are high the 2nd week of April as Robert and Pépin arrive at Red Phillips. The barkeep blanches, but no rematch seems imminent."

*Robert sat down to gamble — three rounds of 50 crowns — and lost two of them:*

**Robert d'Engaux** — "Ah well, lucky in love and all that"

**Narrator** — "Net: -2 SP, -125 crowns. This takes into account the House Divisor"

**Pépin Bonheur** — "Pepin brightens considerable after watching Robert lose — Ahhh, well, time to get a drink and tell stories from our youth" ... "(all of *checks the calendar* ...2 years ago...)"

*Pépin never got his rematch, and Armadine never had to choose again. On the Spring Campaign one game-month later, Pépin's cavalry sweep was Repulsed, and his death roll came up short. His player asked only this: "SO SAD! Please give me a glorious death" ... "Send it to my mother." Robert, his rival of one month, added: "I remember him well." The Narrator obliged with the campaign's most infamous piece of lumber:*

**Narrator** — "Pépin wisely stands far back from the obvious trap. As fragments of teeth and bones of the two scouts rain down on the assembled regiment, he shields his eyes from the downpour of debris. Had he kept them uncovered, he may have seen the smoldering, twirling two-by-four, somehow intact after the explosion, falling down towards where he stands. It whizzes past, but on the backturn makes solid contact with his skull, and Pépin's battle, and life, are over."

*Six plank emojis. The player's fourth character, and the fourth to die — the first by a rapier through the eye on campaign, the second by a thrown cutlass to the throat, the third by beheading at Red Phillips, and now one by airborne carpentry.*
